# Forging - We Priced It Like a Well, Not a Website

## Ground truth beats a spreadsheet blessed by strangers.

The office smelled like dry‑erase marker and burnt coffee. Whiteboard squeak, server fan hum, the kind of air that gets sharp at midnight. We were arguing about pricing—not the deck version, the real one. How do you charge for software that keeps wells honest? Oilfield timelines are measured in seconds and barrels, not slides.

Most companies don’t track what you think they do. Pricing? Overhead? Hidden time sinks? People **assume** systems are running and worry about it later. Price to their fantasy and you’ll drown. We priced to field economics: if we prevented a tank run before a spill, saved a compressor trip, cut a deadhead drive—those weren’t abstractions; they were dollars you could point to by Friday. The price had to fit the well, not the pitch.

Turns out we were pretty fucking close. Later, a big company spent a couple hundred thousand dollars decomposing our model. They came back and said we’d landed exactly where we should be. That felt like a wrench finding true thread—you don’t cheer, you nod, and keep turning.

2010 was the year the math clicked and the year my dad died. Grief doesn’t pause billing, and billing doesn’t care about grief. What I took from him—work while you hurt—kept the company upright. What I added—systems over heroics—kept it from running on fumes.

Today I let AI keep the honesty alive. Feed it messy operator notes, county records, downtime logs; have it surface the quiet places where money leaks. Let it flag when reality and forecasts stop agreeing. But the rule hasn’t changed: **price what reality will thank you for, not what a spreadsheet can be convinced of.**

**One‑line rule:** _If the field can’t feel the value by Friday, the price is a lie._

## AI Playbook — Price From Ground Truth

**Purpose:** Price the thing by the economics of the thing it touches.

**Prompt**

> Write a 500–800 word scene about how you priced a product using the economics of the asset it affects (not competitor prices). Open with the room where the decision happened (sounds, smells). Name two concrete value events (saves, avoids, accelerates). Show how a third party validated or falsified your price later. End with a sticky‑note rule you’d tape above your invoicing screen.

**Constraints**

- Include _two numbers_ you can defend (even as ranges).

- One skeptical voice and your answer.

- No buzzwords you wouldn’t use on a tailgate.
