## The Rule of Charles Cutter

### **Job Never Done Right** :

At 15, I learned perfection was unattainable under my father's scrutiny. His disapproval became my relentless shadow on the farm, a place where change was constant and praise scarce. My ritual became a meticulous scan of chores done, my mind always racing with the question, "What did I overlook?" The best I could hope for was NOT being yelled at for messing up. This environment hardens a person’s mind and heart.

I recall when my Dad would drive into the farm, and I would see him looking around, scanning, hoping to identify something that was missed, left open, or not cleaned up. There is something about the deep fear of sometimes violent consequences that one learns ways to avoid.

I remember coming home with a first-place trophy from a wrestling tournament only to be told that I should have done this or I could have pinned the kid. I made mental notes to myself over those years that I would not repeat this and I would recognize other’s accomplishments.

Even in the triumph of wrestling, where victory should have been mine to savor, he found flaws to pick at. I wrestled not just my opponent but my father's impossible standards. A harsh critic to the end, it took 23 grueling years to wrest a single compliment from his lips.

By the time I was 15, I learned that my dad would find something wrong with everything I did, and it was up to me to remember what he said so that he would not have that reason to be updated. This is very hard to do when living on a farm because things are always changing, and there is always something new to do in a new season.

I learned to deal with Chuck Cutter by scanning everything several times to see if there was anything that he would get mad over. I would asking myself “what did I miss?” i did this with everything. It was a joyous moment sometimes when he returned from the farm and did not have anything to say. My dad could drive in and still be sitting in his truck, and he would find something out of place.

**My coping Mechanism was to work harder to prove him wrong**.

Physical punishment in my home wasn't just an occasional discipline; it was a shadow that could darken any day, regardless of my actions. My body learned to absorb blows, and my mind worked tirelessly to understand and evade them. The resilience I built from these encounters was born from a necessity to survive both mentally and physically.

One of the most grueling tests of this resilience came after a wrestling tournament. Crushed by illness and defeat, I faced an even greater opponent—my father's wrath. Outside the Mapleton gym, as sickness overtook me, it was clear that I was in no shape for competition. Yet, this did not sway my father's expectations or correction method. That day's physical punishment did more than leave bruises; it scarred my definition of fairness and justice.

Later, as I sat isolated in the van, watching my brother claim victory, the sense of injustice grew alongside the resolve not to let the cycle of abuse define me and these long hours of solitude turned reflection into a fortress of determination. I vowed never to be the one to wield such power carelessly or cruelly. Physical abuse might have been the language of discipline I was taught. Still, I chose to articulate my life's lessons through a different lexicon—empathy, understanding, and recognition of others' victories.

These experiences, harsh as they were, equipped me with unanticipated armor. Physical pain became the lesser of evils, as I could shield my mind and remain detached, a dissociative tactic that became my secret survival tool. It's a sad realization to prefer the lashes to the eroding effects of emotional negation.

A shear pin is a mechanical fastener used to prevent damage to machinery during a torque overload situation. It is designed to act as a sacrificial device to protect the machine's other, more expensive, or critical components from becoming damaged.

Here's how it works: the shear pin is made of a material with a known shear strength lower than the strength of the other parts in the assembly. It is inserted into a shaft or a coupling and aligned with another component. Suppose the machinery experiences excessive force or loads because of a jam or overload. In that case, the shear pin breaks or shears off at a predetermined load, disconnecting the drive element from the driven element.

This breakage is a mechanical "fuse," stopping the torque transmission to prevent further damage. Once the jam is cleared or the overload removed, the broken shear pin can be replaced with a new one, and the machine can be put back into operation with minimal downtime and reduced repair costs.

Everyone remembers their first concert, and mine was Genesis on the we can't dance tour. I was in second grade, and in order to earn the ticket to the concert my dad gave CJ and I the opportunity to earn our way to the concert there I was at the Ashland Jaws tournament and facing off with Chad Wright, who later become a close friend of mine. But on this day, he was the enemy, because he stood in front of me, in the first place plaque that every wrestler walks home with after standing at the highest point on the podium, winning the match through a series of back points that I earned, throwing a headlock of all things, something I never did but when the opportunity is there, you take it.
