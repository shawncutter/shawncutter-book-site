# Don’t throw out the baby

_Honor the builder; wedge the problem; make it reversible._

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/e66886c6-77d5-4657-9090-5755ba5d210b-RackMultipart20251006-227-25l1bf.png)

Fluorescent buzz. Downtown Houston boardroom, 12 on their side, 2 on ours. By well count, they were one of the largest oil and gas operators in the country. We walked through our stack. Then SCADA came up.

“I don’t see any future where your system replaces my Wonderware,” Steve said from the back. Not angry—anchored. He wasn’t protecting software. He was protecting a decade of nights.

The SVP of Sales stumbled to recover—buzzwords, half‑answers, air cover. Then our champion cut in, calm: “Fielding Systems delivers the entire service over the cloud in a Microsoft data center. No trucks. No installs.” He held up his iPhone. “Best part? It’s all here.” The room shifted from doubt to curiosity.

We didn’t win that day. We won a year later with a **wedge** —one slice their system couldn’t reach. Augment first, replace last. We changed our playbook: stop threatening people’s work; ask “What pain can’t your current stack solve today?” Then ship that, reversible, with Steve defining success.

**AI tie.** I use AI to **map stakeholders** and propose wedges: who built what, who signs, who vetoes silently. It drafts the acknowledgment paragraph (“Your work got us here”), proposes a reversible test, and forecasts ripple effects. Politics is a dependency graph—instrument it.

_Rule: Respect the craft; change the corner first._

**AI Playbook — Wedge Strategy Canvas**

**Purpose:** Land change without triggering the immune system. **Prompt:** “For a legacy system with an owner, (1) list past wins tied to that owner, (2) name one present pain their tool can’t solve, (3) propose a reversible experiment with rollback, (4) write the acknowledgment paragraph, (5) define 30‑day success.” **Constraints:** No “replace” language • One metric the owner agrees to track • Rollback stated up front.
