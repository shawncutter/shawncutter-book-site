# The Elopement

## We didn't plan a wedding. We planned a Tuesday.

There was an Elvis package.

That sentence does not require additional context to convey everything you need to know about the decision-making process that led to my marriage, but I'll give you some anyway.

Las Vegas, 2006. Carina and I had been together long enough to know what we knew, and what we knew was that we were not people who wanted to organize a large event and stand in the middle of it while everyone watched. We were people who wanted to get it done and then eat somewhere good. The logic was perfectly clear to us, which is a feeling that is either wisdom or delusion, and you can only tell the difference in retrospect.

Two people came with us: my friend Chris, and his now-wife Patty. Four people total. No venue deposits, no seating charts, no arguments about whether the centerpieces matched the napkins. Just a flight to Las Vegas and a small white chapel and an Elvis package.

The chapel was exactly what it needed to be. Small and clean and slightly theatrical in the way that Las Vegas makes everything slightly theatrical — a minor layer of spectacle on top of an otherwise serious transaction. The vows were actual vows. The commitment was actual commitment. The Elvis was adjacent to all of that, which is maybe the only way to do Elvis: adjacent, not central.

The dress broke.

I don't mean it tore dramatically. I mean something structural gave way — a clasp, a seam, the particular engineering of formal wear meeting the practical reality of a small chapel in Las Vegas in July. It was the kind of thing that, if you were the type of person who needed everything to be a certain way, would have turned into a crisis. Carina is not that type of person. We are not those people. We fixed it in the way you fix a dress at a Las Vegas wedding when you don't have the option of calling in a backup: you improvise, you decide that the story of the dress breaking is better than the story of a dress that didn't break, and you get married anyway.

One more detail that I want to say out loud because it is the most Shawn Cutter thing about this entire day: we got married on Carina's birthday. By design. Not romantic accident — deliberate systems thinking applied to the wedding calendar. The logic was clean and I stand by it completely: if the anniversary and the birthday are the same date, there is only one date to remember. The optimization was obvious. The execution was flawless.

We called our parents from the limo.

This was the part that required more explanation than the Elvis. The limo was our transition vehicle — we were riding from the chapel to wherever we were going next, and somewhere in that ride I had the phone and I called my mother, and Carina called her family, and we told them that they had just acquired a son-in-law and a daughter-in-law respectively, and there was a period of adjustment that followed. This is the polite way of saying that not everyone received this information with the equanimity we had hoped for.

It is also worth saying: they came around. Time and grandchildren are remarkably effective at resolving grievances about wedding logistics.

What I know now, looking back at the limo and the phone calls and the small chapel and the four of us and the dress that broke: we did it the right way for us. Not the conventional way. Not the way anyone had imagined it. But the way that was true to who we actually were at twenty-seven and twenty-five in 2006, which was two people who were better at starting things than planning them, better at commitment than ceremony, better at a Tuesday than a production.

I have never regretted the Tuesday.

I've been to plenty of weddings since. Beautiful weddings. Weddings with flowers and photographs and toasts that made people cry in the good way. I enjoyed all of them. I also think there is something quietly powerful about two people who know what they want, standing in a small room with exactly four witnesses and an Elvis impersonator, and doing the thing they came to do.

The dress broke. We got married anyway.

That's the story, and it's the marriage too.

**Cutter Rule:** The ceremony is for the guests. The commitment is for the two of you. Don't confuse which one is the point.
