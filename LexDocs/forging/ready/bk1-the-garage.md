# The Garage

## Every company that means anything started somewhere that didn't look like a company.

The garage was at the back of the Plant City house. Not a large garage. Not a garage that announced anything about what it was going to become. It had concrete floors and no climate control and the kind of fluorescent lights that make everyone look like they haven't slept, which was accurate, because we mostly hadn't.

Two IKEA desks. That was the furniture budget. Not because we were trying to be scrappy about it — we were scrappy about it because we didn't have another option. The desks came flat in boxes and you assembled them yourself with the little IKEA tool and if you stripped a cam lock you figured it out because you were not sending the desk back. You were starting a company.

Jeff was there first. Jeff Taton. Then Mike Huff. Those are the names I say when people ask who built Fielding Systems, because the honest answer is that I started it and they built it, and there's a meaningful difference between those two things. I could generate the idea, hold the vision, make the calls. Jeff and Mike could actually make the thing work. Between us, we covered enough ground that it started to feel like something real.

The first customer was Cutter Oil.

I know how that sounds. It sounds like I gave myself a cushion, a guaranteed first win. Maybe. But it was also just true that the people who were most willing to bet on a company out of a garage in Plant City were the people who already knew the person who started it, and the person who knew me best was my own family. Cutter Oil needed what we were building. We needed a first customer. The logic was simple enough not to require apology.

What I remember about those early days isn't the product or the roadmap or the conversations about where this was going. What I remember is the *specific texture* of it — the smell of the garage in August, the way the light changed in the late afternoon, Jeff's voice when something broke at midnight and we needed to diagnose it before anyone woke up in California and noticed. The particular intensity of a team of three in a garage at 2 in the morning who know that they are either building something or they're not, and who have enough pride and enough stubbornness to not yet allow themselves to find out which one it is.

We were building an operations platform for oilfield companies — a way to manage field tickets, crew dispatch, job tracking, all the administrative weight of moving people and equipment through a landscape that is actively trying to defeat you. The problem was real because I'd lived inside it through Cutter Oil. I'd ridden in the passenger seat of my father's truck listening to him work deals and dispatch crews and track jobs with a system that was held together by paper and phone calls and institutional memory. I knew what the gaps looked like from the inside.

That knowledge was the product. Not just the code.

The code was important. But the thing that made the code useful was that it came from someone who had sat in that passenger seat, who understood the language — who knew what a day rate was, what a call-out meant, why field guys didn't fill out paperwork the way office guys filled out paperwork. You could build software that was technically correct and completely wrong at the same time if you didn't know the terrain.

We knew the terrain.

That's what gets compressed when people tell the startup story later. They say *we started in a garage* and it sounds humble, it sounds gritty, it sounds like the origin myth. What they don't say is that the garage is not the hardship — the hardship is the terrifying, exhilarating gap between the version of the thing that exists in your head and the version that actually runs on a server. That gap is where you find out who you are.

Jeff and Mike found out they were exactly who I'd thought they were: the kind of people who close the gap.

We eventually moved out of the garage. The desks got replaced. The company grew into something that a garage couldn't contain. But I still know the weight of a flat IKEA box and what it means to build a desk at the beginning of something you can't fully see yet.

You just start. Then you figure out what you're starting.

**Cutter Rule:** The address doesn't make the company. The people in the room make the company. Find two of the right ones and the rest is just square footage.
