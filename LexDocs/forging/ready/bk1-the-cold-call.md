# The Cold Call

## You can't sell oil and gas software to oil and gas people if you sound like you've never been in a truck.

The oilfield has its own language.

Not metaphorically — there is actual vocabulary. Field tickets. Day rates. Call-outs. Standby pay. AFEs. The gap between the tool pusher and the company man and who answers to whom and when. If you walked into a conversation with an operations manager at a mid-size oil and gas company and you talked like a software person — if you said *platform* and *workflow optimization* and *digital transformation* in a row — you'd lost before you'd started. The guy across the table had been up since four in the morning. He had mud on his boots that he'd tracked in and hadn't noticed. He was not looking for transformation. He was looking for someone who knew what his actual day looked like.

I knew what his actual day looked like.

That was the whole thing. That was the asset I had that nobody else in the room had, and the question was whether I was smart enough to use it.

I'd grown up inside Cutter Oil. Not as an executive, not as an observer — as a kid in the passenger seat, listening. I knew the dispatch problems. I knew why field guys didn't fill out paperwork the way office guys wanted field guys to fill out paperwork. I knew the difference between a problem that was a process problem and a problem that was a trust problem, which look identical from the outside and require completely different solutions. I knew the language because I'd heard it since I was eight years old with the radio on low in a truck driving through Ohio fields.

So when I made a cold call into an oil and gas company, I didn't start with the product.

I started with the problem. The specific, textured, field-level problem. I'd say: *You've got thirty guys out there and you don't know where half of them are until they call in, and when they call in the ticket's already a day behind and you're chasing paper before you can bill.* I'd say it the way someone says something they've seen firsthand, not the way someone says something they've read in a case study.

The conversation changed.

The operations manager would lean forward slightly, or the line would go quiet in the way lines go quiet when someone is actually listening instead of waiting to respond. Because I'd described his morning. I hadn't pitched at him — I'd described his Tuesday to him accurately, and now he was wondering whether I was the guy who could fix it.

That's the cold call. That's all the cold call ever is: demonstrate that you understand their world better than they expected you to, and then let the conversation go where it needs to go.

Cold calling is not a numbers game in the way people say it's a numbers game. Yes, volume matters. You have to make enough of them. But the ones that convert don't convert because you called a hundred people — they convert because when you reached the right person, you said the right thing in the right language, and the right language in oilfield sales is not software language. It's field language. It's the language of the truck and the ticket and the call-out at midnight when something breaks and someone needs to be somewhere in four hours.

I'd been in training for that language for fifteen years before I ever needed to sell anything.

The passenger seat was a classroom. The radio on low was a curriculum. My father's voice working a deal while we drove through Ohio fields was the best sales education I ever got, and nobody called it that, because it didn't look like education. It looked like riding in a truck.

Most of what I know, I learned by riding in a truck with someone who knew more than me.

The lesson here isn't about cold calling. It's about what you accumulate when you're paying attention in the passenger seat, before you know what you're going to need it for.

**Cutter Rule:** Learn the language of the room before you try to change what happens in it. Vocabulary is the first door. If you don't have the key, nothing else matters.
