# The Last Flight

## I got my pilot's license in 2008. My father died in 2010. He got to see me fly.

This is the thing I hold onto.

He got to see me fly.

I earned my license at 41 hours — which is on the low end, and I'm not being modest, it's just fact. Forty-one hours of training to solo, cross-country, written exam, checkride. And somewhere in those first flights after the license, I took my father up.

I did the full pre-flight inspection the way I always did it, except this time I talked through it. Every last detail. What I was looking for, where something could break, how the process worked. It was no different than walking a piece of equipment before you run it — you check that things are tight, nothing needs grease. If you upkeep a machine, it runs for damn near forever. Parts have to be replaced occasionally, but all of those are fixable. A plane is no different. The reason air travel is so safe is the details, the same inspection, the same way, every single time.

Chuck would have understood this. He'd spent his whole life around equipment — oilfield equipment, farm equipment, machines that would kill you if you didn't respect them. Walking him through the pre-flight was the first time in my life I was the one explaining the machine to him, instead of the other way around.

He was super quiet.

That's the detail I keep coming back to. Chuck Cutter — the man of velocity, the man of 175 laps at Michigan Speedway, the man who put his three-year-old on a dirt bike — was quiet in the right seat of my Cessna 182. Not nervous exactly. Taking it all in. Aware, I think, that he was in a small plane and could die. Aware that his son was the one between him and the ground.

So naturally, I did some zero-G's.

That's where you push the nose down hard and everything goes weightless for a second — your stomach lifts, anything loose in the cabin floats, and for a brief moment you are falling together with the airplane instead of being carried by it. I knew he'd like it. The thrill-seeker in him — that was always there. But I think it spooked him some. Which was the point. That was fun. Getting to spook Chuck Cutter, the man who'd spooked me my entire childhood with silos and dirt bikes and the particular intensity of his attention — getting to be the one who made him grip the armrest for once. That reversal meant something.

We landed. We went for wings.

I can't remember the name of the place — some barbecue spot we fell in love with, just incredible wings. But the wings were always the thing. That's what brought us together, every time, wherever we were. BW3 in Ohio. Sports bars off I-71 in Cleveland. Whatever place was closest that had buffalo wings and a table. We'd sit and talk about the flavor and the heat and order another round. It was never about the food exactly. It was about having a thing — a reliable, shared, low-stakes thing that two men who weren't always good at talking to each other could sit inside and not have to explain.

Wings after flying.

That's the image. That's what I have.

Two years later, at 3:30 in the morning, CJ would call to tell me Chuck had a heart attack. He died on November 12th, 2010, at fifty-four years old. I would fly to an airport with sunglasses on and not take them off.

But before that — before the call, before the wall, before the sunglasses — there was a flight where my father was in the right seat and I was in the left. Where I walked him through a pre-flight the way he'd walked me through a thousand things on the farm. Where I spooked him with zero-G's and he got quiet in a way that meant he was alive inside a moment and not trying to control it.

And then we went for wings. Because that's what we did.

I don't know if I'll go back to flying. The question sits on a shelf I haven't opened. But I know I put my father in the sky and he got to see me fly. And I know we sat somewhere after, with wings on the table, and it was good.

That's not nothing.

That's everything.

**Cutter Rule:** The things you build in the window before someone is gone — take the flight, make the call, order the wings. The window is always smaller than you think.
