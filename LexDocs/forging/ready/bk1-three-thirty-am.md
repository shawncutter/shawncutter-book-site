# 3:30 AM

## The world stops. Everything mutes. Time moves differently.

The phone rang at 3:30 in the morning and I knew before I answered it.

Not in the way people say they knew — retroactively, once they have information to assign the feeling to. I mean the actual knowing. The specific, cellular recognition that something has ended. The body figured it out before the phone reached my ear.

CJ was on the line. *Dad had a heart attack.* They were doing CPR. He said it the way you say a thing when you're trying to keep your own voice from collapsing while you say it. Flat and careful and very fast.

I was in Florida. A thousand miles from anything I could do.

I don't remember putting the phone down. I remember being on the floor. My back against the wall and I'd slid down it the way you only slide down a wall in the movies, except it wasn't a movie, it was my actual body refusing to hold itself upright. My head in my knees. The room very dark. The kind of dark that exists after a phone call at 3:30 in the morning when you are trying to figure out whether the world you knew still exists or whether it's a different world now and you're just catching up.

He was going to be okay. That's what I told myself while I found the flights. You always think they're going to be okay. It's not denial, exactly — it's the mind preserving the operational part of you long enough to make the decisions that need to be made. Book the flight. Get to the airport. Get on the plane. Think about whether he's okay or not after you're in the air.

He wasn't okay.

He died at 5:15 in the morning.

By the time I landed, there was no arriving in time. There was just arriving.

I don't remember much about the airport except that I kept my sunglasses on. I know I did because I know I never took them off. The sunglasses were the only strategy I had. They were a wall between my face and a world that was continuing to exist in complete indifference to what had just happened to my family. People were getting coffee. People were on their phones. People were rolling suitcases. All the airports in America were operating exactly as designed, and none of them knew that Chuck Cutter had died at 5:15 in the morning.

The sunglasses stayed on.

There is something that happens to time when someone you love dies. It's not grief, not yet — grief is something you find later, in quieter moments, when you're not still moving through airports. What happens first is that time becomes *thick*. The space between one moment and the next gets wider. You notice things you don't usually notice — the color of a gate number sign, the sound of a jet bridge closing. You are intensely present and also completely untethered. You are looking at the world through glass.

Chuck would have been annoyed by the sunglasses indoors. He had opinions about things like that. He would have said something and I would have given it back to him and he would have gone sideways into a joke and the room would have relaxed. That's what would have happened if the last thirty-six hours had been different.

Instead I kept the sunglasses on and I got to wherever I was going and then at some point I took them off and the world was still there and I had to start being in it.

He was fifty-four years old. It was November 12th, 2010.

There is no sufficient number of years. There is no version of this where the call comes at an acceptable time. But fifty-four is not enough. Fifty-four is a man who still had things to figure out, and I know he was figuring them out, because I watched it happening — the softening, the patience, the adapting. He would have kept going. He would have gotten to whatever the next version of Chuck Cutter was.

We didn't get to see it.

What I learned later — more than a decade later — was something about myself that I couldn't see in the immediate aftermath. I had moved through the whole thing: the call, the wall, the airport, the sunglasses, the weeks and months after. I had functioned. I had kept building. I had not come apart. And I told myself that was strength, or resilience, or just who I was.

What it actually was, I came to understand, was how small my emotional dimension had become. Not by choice. By design. By childhood. The specific design of growing up in that house, with the belt and the counting and the years of learning that feeling things loudly was not safe — that had quietly built a protective wall between me and my own interior. I was on the other side of it without knowing the wall was there.

Chuck's death was probably the hardest thing I have ever gone through emotionally. I know this because when I finally got close enough to look at it directly, years later, the size of it was larger than anything I'd let myself feel at the time. The sunglasses didn't just stay on at the airport. They stayed on, in various forms, for much longer than that.

Some things you leave on. Some distances you keep between yourself and the fact of the thing. But eventually the distance collapses — not on your timeline, and not gently. It collapses when it's ready.

**Cutter Rule:** You don't get to choose how the story ends. You only get to choose how you carry it.
