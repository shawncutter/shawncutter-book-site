# The Dave Problem

## Every deal has a Dave. Dave is not actually the problem. The equation around Dave is the problem.

It was horrible.

I want to say that up front because the version of this story that sounds good at a dinner party — *I navigated the politics, found the right angle, closed the deal* — is not what it felt like from inside. From inside, Dave McCarty was trying to kill the Quorum deal, and what I had to do about it was ugly and exhausting and required a level of face-management that I do not naturally possess.

Here's what was actually happening: Dave was making triple what anyone else was making.

Not slightly more. Not a premium for seniority. Triple. And the person who was most aware of this, and most unable to sit with it, was Jeff Taton — my right hand, my CTO, the guy who had been with me from the IKEA desks in the garage, who had built the thing alongside me through every midnight fix and every cold call conversion. Jeff was looking at the compensation structure inside Quorum and seeing a man who was getting paid three times what the people who actually built the product were getting paid, and it was not sitting well.

That's the real Dave problem. Not that Dave wanted to kill the deal — that was a symptom. The problem was that the equation was wrong. Dave's economics were out of balance with his contribution relative to the team that built Fielding, and that imbalance was creating toxicity that threatened the whole transaction.

So I had to do two things simultaneously that felt contradictory:

Go around Dave. And maintain face with him while I did it.

This is the part that doesn't make it into the LinkedIn version of the story. The actual mechanics of navigating a deal saboteur inside an acquiring company is not a clean intellectual exercise. It's social engineering. You smile in the meetings. You acknowledge his concerns with apparent sincerity. You let him feel heard. And then you work every channel around him — the executives who actually control the decision, the board members who see the strategic value, the people who can rebalance the equation so the deal makes sense for everyone, including the people it wasn't making sense for.

The adjustments I had to make were about money. That's what it always comes down to. Dave's compensation was a problem, and the solution was to restructure the arrangement so that the people who had actually built the product — Jeff, specifically — weren't staring at a pay gap that made the whole thing feel like a betrayal. You can't tell your CTO *trust me, this is the right move for us* while he's watching someone with a fraction of his contribution collect three times his check. The math doesn't work. The loyalty doesn't survive that math.

So I rebalanced it. Quietly, carefully, with the specific kind of negotiation that happens in side conversations and adjusted term sheets and the slow, deliberate reshaping of a deal structure that started wrong and needed to be made right before it could close.

Dave didn't know exactly how much of this was happening. That was the point. The face had to hold. The relationship had to look functional in every room where people were watching, because the moment it looked dysfunctional, the deal would have a new problem on top of the old problem.

It closed on March 23rd, 2015. The Aria. Las Vegas. Two thousand people at the event.

Here's the thing about Dave that makes this more complicated than a villain story: Dave was great at sales. Legitimately great. He had a pipeline of customers and the company was growing fast under that pipeline. He explained things — how deals worked, how objections moved, how you read a room. I asked him every question I could think of, because I wanted to learn everything about sales, and I was honest enough with myself to see the mistakes I was making. Dave taught me things I still use.

But Dave was also content. Content with the trajectory, content with his equity — equity that had been *given* to him, not earned through the building — because if the company just kept growing the way it was growing, he'd make more money. That was the calculus. Dave didn't want the deal because the deal changed the math on his free equity. And that's where I learned one of the most expensive lessons of my career: people who are given equity do not appreciate it the same way people who build equity do. They just don't. The psychology is different. The ownership is different. The skin in the game is different.

I would make this mistake again. More than once. It would take me a couple more rounds of watching someone treat gifted equity like found money before I finally wised up. By the time I started EnergiAcres, I had learned. I've moved slower than I would like — but I've also stopped getting involved with people who aren't the right fit, who don't align with my values. That's the Dave tax. You pay it in time and pain and then you stop paying it.

Jeff was watching. Jeff had earned the right to watch. And the deal only worked because I made sure the equation got fixed, even if fixing it meant playing a game I didn't enjoy.

The deal doesn't have to be clean. It has to be right.

**Cutter Rule:** When the equation is wrong, fix the equation. And when you give someone equity, make sure they earned it — because the ones who didn't will be the first to fight you when the math changes.
