# The 40% Ask

## Blood is thicker than water and considerably more expensive than equity.

CJ wanted 40 percent of Fielding Systems.

Not a little piece. Not a finder's fee. Not an advisory stake. Forty percent — almost half the company — at a point when I had built it from nothing, when the IKEA desks were still fresh in my memory, when Jeff and Mike and I had bled the early version of it into existence through a series of nights that made normal hours feel like a foreign concept.

I heard the number and I did what I always do when someone says something that doesn't compute: I ran the calculation. Not the emotional calculation — that one happens automatically, whether you want it to or not. The business calculation. What 40 percent of Fielding was worth at the time versus what CJ had put in. What it would mean for the cap table going forward. What the number implied about how he valued what had already been built versus what he expected to contribute.

The math didn't work.

This is the version of the story where I tell you I said no calmly and we moved on. That's not the version. The version is that it created one of the harder conversations of my life, because CJ is my brother, and because there is a specific kind of damage that can only happen between people who know each other from before they knew anything — who learned the belt method in the same house, who rode in the same truck, who called across the same valley to the same cousins when they needed to know someone was there.

You don't argue about equity with a stranger the same way you argue about it with someone who remembers the same things you remember.

I drove that night. Hartford to Avon, or some variation of it — one of those stretches of interstate where you put a destination in and your hands are on the wheel but your brain is somewhere else entirely. I was coding while I drove. Not on the laptop — in my head. I was debugging the CJ problem the way I debug any problem: breaking it into components, tracing the logic, trying to find where the error was.

The error wasn't in the math. The math was clear. The error was in the expectation — the sense that family proximity to a thing earns a stake in it. That being around something, believing in someone, being willing to say *I'm in* late in the process entitles you to terms that don't reflect the risk that was actually taken. That's not CJ being wrong, exactly. That's a very human way to think about how things should work. But it's not how equity works, and confusing the two is how you destroy both the company and the relationship.

I'd watched my father navigate business with family his entire career. Cutter Oil was a family operation. The whole enterprise was built on the idea that blood was the deepest trust. I believed in that and still believe in it. But I also learned, in that car on that interstate, that family trust and business terms are not the same document. You can love someone completely and still not give them 40 percent of the thing you built.

I want to be honest about something here, because this book is honest or it's nothing: CJ and I had history that ran deeper than equity. Before I was ever hit by a car and had forty surgeries, I'd already accumulated eighty-seven stitches. Half of those came from CJ. Including thirty-two of them. Including a rock thrown at my head, on purpose.

That's the brother I was sitting in the car thinking about on that drive. Not a stranger. Not a business partner I could evaluate cleanly. A brother who'd put me in stitches — literally — and who I'd fought with in the way that brothers on a farm in Ohio fight, which is to say physically, territorially, and with the specific brutality that comes from sharing a house where the rules were enforced with a belt and everyone was learning violence as a language.

We found our way through it then. We found our way through the belt and the van and the years of territory wars that brothers fight before they know how to be anything other than brothers. I thought we'd find our way through this too.

We didn't.

CJ hasn't talked to me since roughly 2017. He blocked me — not metaphorically, actually blocked my number. It wasn't just the equity. It was how he was treating our siblings, and I told him what I thought about it. He didn't want to hear it from me. He hasn't wanted to hear anything from me since.

My mom brought us together once, thinking we could collaborate on a pipeline and gas storage deal, selling the field. But I was still blocked. There was no way to communicate. Even in 2024 — still blocked.

He's a grandfather now. I wish him the best. If he ever called me, needed me, I would be there. But I don't try to change other people. I don't spend any cognitive energy on that at all. It is what it is.

I drove a long time that night in 2009. I'm still driving, in a way. The road just got longer than I expected.

If you are starting something and someone asks you for a number that doesn't match the work they've done, the answer has to be based on contribution, not on relationship. The relationship doesn't change — it outlasts the equity conversation if you handle it right. But the equity conversation has to be honest, or you're not doing either one of you any favors.

The company has to make sense on its own terms. And sometimes the relationship doesn't survive the honesty.

**Cutter Rule:** Family relationships and business terms are two separate agreements. Keep them separate. Mixing them doesn't make either stronger — it makes both fragile. And sometimes keeping them separate is the thing that costs you the relationship. Do it anyway. The alternative costs you both.
