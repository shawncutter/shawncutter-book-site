# **BK:** Chapter 2: **The Day the Earth Shook**

Growing up in the country, our four wheelers, ATV’s they are called today, were our means of transportation. In order to fuel these up, we filled our gas cans up at the Boreman farm, which is several miles away. the Boreman farm was also the very first Wells that my dad drilled back in the early 80s and develop the friendship with the father and his triplet sons Rick, Randy, and Rod. Rod was the father of my best friend at the time Tony Boreman.

It was Tuesday, October 29, and I had so much to look forward to, including the pizza party the next day for our championship win in soccer, which I was the goalie. But on this day, in a trip that would typically include my brother driving the four wheeler and myself riding on the back to assist, I was taking this trip alone. My older brother CJ insisted that I go pick up gas why he wait at home. Since I did not get to drive the four wheeler that much on a trip like this, I was eager to go.

I was more of a football player and half of the time and made fun of anyone playing soccer. However, I joined the soccer team as a way to get out of a detention that I got in for getting in trouble at lunchtime that put me on the green bench which was strategically placed right outside the cafeteria, so that every kid leaving the cafeteria, heading to recess would see you on the bench. Needless to say when the art teacher who is also the soccer coach saw me there and asked if I wanted to get off the bench I gladly accepted. I was the goalie because I got to wear a cooler shirt and better gloves and I managed to keep most of the balls out of the goal, hence the championship that.

The Boreman farm sat at the bottom of a long driveway that went down the hill on the one side of the farm, and while I was driving the ATV, pulling a little red wagon behind it the pin fell out of the hitch and the empty wagon, and it's dug into the ground and it would take me approximately 20 minutes to go down to the farm and retrieve a new pin and get it hooked back up. To make manners worse when I got down to the gas tank with the wagon and empty gas cans I was told that they were out of gas and I would have to come back in another day, it was about this time that I realized why my older brother had told me that he wanted to stay back and that was because my mom had said she was coming home and would be taking us in town and we had the opportunity to get McDonald's, which was a really big deal for us.

I decided that the best decision was to leave the wagon and empty gas cans at the farm and ride the four wheeler home as fast as I could so that I would be home in time to meet my mom to go in town. Removing the wagon allowed me to drive twice as fast and would be one of those faithful decisions that one looks back in questions, could I have done it differently? I imagine the dust storm, that the four wheeler threw up as a raced up the driveway and towards Rice Hill Road, where I would slow down, but never really needed to fully stop because there was rarely traffic on this road.

As I eased up on the throttle and turned left onto the road, a little voice said look, but it was too late. I remember time slowing down, and I could see through the windshield this year terror on the lady’s face who was about to run over a 10-year-old who swerved left as fast as she could and ran off the road down into a field, but the car still collided with a four-wheeler, destroying it on impact along with my foot.

I was throwing approximately 20 feet from the point of impact where my helmet would be shattered, resulting in stitches on my forehead, but the real damage was to my left foot, which was pinned between the foot peg the motor in the car bumper. My left foot lost that battle. I would wake up in the middle of the road, and the lady who hit me was not out of the car yet. I remember her walking up onto the road, and I was screaming, “go get someone!” Neighbors had heard about the accident and came to help. I was no stranger to stitches and injuries, but this was a different ball game.

When I pulled my sock off, I remember the skin flipping over my toes as my foot had been degloved. the blood that was squirting out of my foot was a site that I'll never forget, as I held my leg up on my calf to keep my foot off of the road. with every heartbeat, a new squirt of blood landed on the road.  
  
Mike Krueger’s Dad who worked at a nearby paint factory was on the scene within minutes and I recognized him. I was crying and extremely distraught, repeating over and over that I wouldn't be able to walk again and I wouldn't be able to wrestle again. Joe Rice, who had graduated with my mom in school, was also the first EMT on the scene. By the time my mom arrived on the scene they already had me in the squad and they would not allow her to see me or ride in the squad to the hospital. My mom would follow the squad to the hospital and what was I'm sure the longest trip to Wooster ever.

I don't remember my mom at the hospital, but I do remember when my dad walked through the emergency room door and stood above my bed. Which one of the first times that I remember seeing concern or fear in his eyes and that's probably because now as a father, I now know the feeling of helplessness when your child is hurt. And the thing is, my dad knew exactly what it felt like to be on the other side, laying in the bed, because he had been there at the age of 16. And everyone in the family knew about his accident, and everyone would surely know about mine. The emergency doctors were go on to tell me that I needed to have a catheter put in and I was going to be life flighted to Akron Children's Hospital. The one thing that I was not gonna allow at that time a catheter so I proceeded to pee every time they tried to put it in, and eventually they gave up that I know they would simply take care of that problem when I was asleep.

I do not remember much of the flight, but I do remember getting wheeled to the helicopter. I woke up two days later in Akron children's ICU with lots of monitoring and beeping around me. I immediately try to go back to sleep, hoping that it was all a dream. My parents are gone home because the staff said they did not know when I would wake up and there was nothing they could do there. The nurses informed me that the doctor would be in later that morning, to cut the bandages off and inspect the damage. All I could keep thinking about was the look of how bad my foot was in the blood, thinking that there was no way that they could've put it back together and I would see incredible damage to my foot.

As a doctor was trimming the bandages off, I remember saying that it hurt because I didn't want them to cut it off, but they proceeded, and as the bandage came off, a terrible smell filled the room. It was clear that the medical staff understood with this meant at the time, but the full gravity of the situation did not sink it to me. It would take 10 surgeries to cut away the dead and dying tissue, along with extreme periods of intense pain, resulting in the eventual use of a morphine drip. Often times when a patient is in pain they give them a button they can press however in some cases when you press the button, the morphine puts you to sleep, and it takes too long to wake up and the next time you receive that same dosage, it's not enough.

Please skip the next few pages to avoid gruesome descriptions of what it was like in the hospital during these times. The thing about gangrene is that if it is not cut away, it will infect the rest of the tissue surrounding the wound, and in my case this would've meant a certain loss of my left foot. The doctors described that if this had happened five years prior the only way to bring blood flow to my left foot and the tissue would be to so the 2 feet together, but this was 1990 and we had options. It was a relatively new technique in which a muscle is taken from another part of the body. Bloood vessels are spliced from the leg and into the model muscle, providing the necessary tissue needed between bones and ligaments and outer skin.

For my situation, this meant the extraction of the latissimus, dorsal muscle from my back along with large skin graft donor sites from all of this was planned out between the surgeries that were used to extricate the dead flash from my foot. And that process involved surgery after surgery to cut away and what would be the most painful experience, wet gauze pads were applied and allowed to dry. Do you know how when you have a Band-Aid, and it sticks to the wound and pulls the scab off; it's like that but my whole foot. I recall, holding the handrails on my bed as they would pull the gauze pads off in the dead flesh with it. The pain was so unbearable that I would pass out in the middle of it; the body safety mechanisms to keep it from experiencing in recalling the absolute worst of times.

The muscle flap surgery whiskey graphs was slated to take between 14 and 15 hours and this would be the only surgery that I recall significant concern on my part about the potential of not waking up. we had planned for the surgery to use my left side for the muscle and skin donations. However, an audible was called by my doctors during the surgery to take it from my right side because it would've taken too long to remove and apply it to my left foot. One of the comments made after the fact was that I wouldn't be a professional baseball player or rock climber after the surgery because I was missing that muscle.

They often sometimes share a room with another patient most of who were able to walk walk in therefore could travel down the hallway to the playroom where Nintendo's and other games existed. But for someone in the wheelchair and stuck in bed, these were off the table. I had nurses I would befriend and many of them I would revisit years later and every one of them would remember me. I remember the first time I was in the wheelchair and got to travel to the playroom, and being very disappointed that this was the first time I would see it because there were a lot of games that I could've asked for that I was unaware of.

No 10-year-old should experience such physical pain and emotional isolation simultaneously. Little did I know that my road to recovery would be years in the making. I spent part of that time on mental games and other parts tracing back the scene and the sequences that led up to the accident. Asking myself the question what did I do wrong and how did I end up at that place at that time? Two seconds earlier, and I have a close call or I simply passed the lady on the road heading home. and two seconds later, that lady is already passed the driveway, and I didn't even see her.

In the end, I would deduce that chaos reigned on me that day with a pin falling out of the wagon. That event cost me at least 20 minutes and the fact that there was no gasoline meant that I didn't spend a 30 minutes filling up those cans and I didn't have to drive home slow because I had a wagon full of gas on the back. And I had no wagon and I was able to drive as fast as I could, after all, I like to race four wheelers for fun.

I would enjoy many visitors to the hospital, including family and friends and also the pastor from our church. I did not really know the guy as we were a family that only went to church on major holidays, because we worked instead. Work I would later find out is part of the Cutter ethos. I would say I was a believer in God prior to the accident, but that would all change after my own personal realization that the God that I had been taught about could not exist. so when the pastor come to talk to me about God's plan, I was a 10-year-old kid that said that God doesn't exist, and I don't wanna hear about a guy that has a plan that put me here. I very much caught. The pastor offguard, and he chose to sit in the room and comfort me with his presence and not say anything that I recall.

I received many cards and letters from classmates, and even from the surrounding community. My fifth grade teacher, Mrs. Lang also brought my homework and help tutor me as I was off school for months. since I was not able to go to the playroom, my entertainment was books and a Nintendo game.
