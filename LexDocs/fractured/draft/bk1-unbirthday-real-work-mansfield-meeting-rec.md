# Un‑Birthday, Real Work: Mansfield Meeting Recap

## Promise less. Publish more.

The room smelled like dry‑erase and hallway coffee. Outside: late‑October Ohio, rain threatening the sidewalks. Inside: the mayor of Mansfield, the city engineer, the planner, and the economic development director. No partners at the table—on purpose. This one was about the city first.

I opened by telling them it was my **un‑birthday** —the accident anniversary that reset my clock when I was a kid. They thanked me for the work I’m doing and, in that quiet human way, for surviving. Those moments matter. They help me move forward.

**What we covered—plain and direct**

- **Reality check:** The State of Ohio isn’t going to fund city‑scale infrastructure for an energy campus. If we want this to stand up on schedule, **we** have to stand it up—through the partnerships we’re already making on **power, gas, and infrastructure**. Ohio is also not offering incentives to the most important industry in the history of the human species. 

- **How we’ll handle it:** Identify **the city’s needs** that block the project (permits, taps, subs, roads, water, workforce), and **solve those directly** to unlock the site. No hand‑waving, no “TBD’s.”

- **What makes us different:** The mayor is right to focus on jobs. A traditional data center is jobs‑light once it’s built. Our model is **jobs‑dense** because it stacks **energy + food + infrastructure intelligence** : generation, microgrids, greenhouse loops, district heat, construction trades, controls techs, growers, and maintenance. Factories of infrastructure that keep hiring long after ribbon‑cutting.

**The posture we committed to**

- **Power‑first, land‑second:** Behind‑the‑meter gas with batteries to stabilize the feeder, microgrids that island politely, and a clean interop with the utility.

- **Open standards:** Nobody gets held hostage by a single vendor.

- **Public KPIs:** Post a monthly scoreboard—**jobs (local %), paid trainees, local vendor spend, SAIDI/MAIFI impact, heat & CO₂ reuse, water recirculation, hotline closures**.

- **One weekly table:** The four city leads + our team. Decisions only happen when the owners are in the room.

**What we asked from the city**

- A **permits matrix** with owners and dates.

- A single **issue log** with a 72‑hour escalation rule. Silence triggers a hand‑off.

- Introductions to **workforce partners** so paid seats can start on time.

**What we’ll shoulder**

- The **infrastructure lift** the state won’t fund—through our partnerships on power, gas, and civil.

- The **community dividend** on day one: neighbor‑circuit refrigeration, greenhouse warmth, and public reporting.

- The **proof of difference** the mayor asked for: a jobs plan that outperforms a data‑center‑only build.

This is not a speech. It’s a schedule and a promise: **time is borrowed; purpose is not**.

**Next 14 days**

- Lock utility pre‑app follow‑ups; publish **tap + substation** alternatives (A/B/C) with timelines.

- Draft **MOUs** with training partners; post paid seat counts and start dates.

- Stand up the **public KPI template** and the **community hotline** ; run a 48‑hour responsiveness drill.

- Print the **permits matrix** and hang it in both rooms—the city’s and ours.

_Rule: Promise less. Publish more._
