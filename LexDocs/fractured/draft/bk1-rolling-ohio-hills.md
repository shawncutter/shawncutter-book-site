# Rolling Ohio Hills

## The gas run that split my life in two.

The Honda 125cc rattled like a coffee can full of bolts. I was ten, knees squeezing the tank, red gas cans in the back of a wagon. Northeast Ohio in late fall smells like corn dust and woodsmoke, like metal left outside all night. The road was a ribbon between fields, frost still in the ditch shadows. Out here, errands weren’t errands. They were competency tests. Go get gas. Don’t spill it. Be back before the light fades.

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/12b729ea-fb18-42d9-811a-c8a8d5d4c92f-RackMultipart20250928-190-6ftgft.png)

I remember the buzz in my hands, the wind needling my eyes, the farm rising out of the hills like it always did — pasture fields, the long driveway, the feeling of belonging that comes from being part of something bigger. Bummer, no gas, leave the wagon here, come back tomorrow.

I thought about a soccer party later. I thought about McDonald’s. I wondered why my brother had told me to go by myself. I thought about speed. I leaned into the turn onto the roadway.

Then everything unclipped.

Gravel leapt. Handlebars snapped sideways. My stomach went hollow, then the world went slow, then it went bright. Metal, dirt, the kind of sound you feel in your teeth. People talk about blood like it’s red. It’s also heat. Lifeblood. The driveway that should have carried me home carried me someplace else.

Hospitals have their own weather. Before I was life-flighted to Akron Children’s, I spent a few hours in the Wooster Emergency room, which was sterile and humming, a climate made of beeps and shoe squeaks. My dad came in once and tried to look like he always did: certain, loud, in charge. I saw fear — real fear, the kind that doesn’t ask permission. That’s when a kid part of me retired early. If he couldn’t save me, I had to learn how to save myself. I don’t remember arriving in Akron I drifted off for days. Tubes in, numbers blinking, adults whispering in the hall.&nbsp;

Thirty-two surgeries. I don’t list them for drama; I list them because that’s how my calendar looked. New scars like county roads on a map I didn’t ask for. The pain became a room I lived in. I got good at small talk with nurses and memorizing ceiling tiles. You can learn anything if you’re stuck long enough. I learned patience that tasted like metal. I learned how to breathe through fire.

Back at school, crutches made a metronome on the tile floors. Kids stared and then overcorrected into not looking at all. I figured out a trick: if I moved fast, no one could study my limp. If I performed—grades, jokes, competence—the eyes stayed off the broken parts. Work became camouflage. If I did enough, maybe I could outrun what was obvious.

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/35c69615-ba91-4db2-a625-9dd018dabe73-RackMultipart20250928-190-zirtwa.png)

The farm didn’t bend. Steers don’t care about your stitches. Pumps don’t care about your limp. Chores don’t file for extensions. I hauled hay with pain biting my calves and learned that “fair” isn’t a metric on a gauge. The farm taught me the first systems lesson I ever understood: reality is indifferent. Build for that. Shit will break. Plan for it.

On equipment you use a shear pin—the designed weak point that snaps to protect the rest. Bodies don’t come with those. When I broke, it was bone and tendon, and nothing downstream got spared. That’s why, later, I built companies the opposite way: create sacrificial parts. Let ideas fail fast; let processes take the hit so people don’t have to. Bodies should not be anyone’s surge protector.

People expect a clean bow here—gratitude, a tidy flip to “everything happens for a reason.” I don’t believe in tidy. I believe in true. Pain didn’t make me special. It made me specific. It gave me an instrument panel for suffering and taught me how to read it without panicking. It taught me that consistency is not bravado; it’s rehab. Today I lead like a kid who learned to count victories in millimeters: one turn of the wrench, one careful step, one more day.

This series is me refusing to limp in silence. It’s me handing you the parts list: what broke, what I replaced, and what I designed so it doesn’t break the same way again.

### AI Playbook — Ship One True Scene

**Purpose:** A reusable self‑exploration prompt for anyone to surface a formative memory and turn it into a publishable scene.

**Prompt:**

> Write the most honest 300–600 word memory you still feel in your body. Open with a sensory detail (smell, sound, texture). Show one turning point. End with one rule you live by now that came from that moment. Keep the language plain enough to say out loud.

**Constraints:**

- One scene, one insight.

- Present tense sensory details; past tense narrative is fine.

- No metaphors you wouldn’t use in conversation.

- Close with a single sentence principle.

**Optional follow‑ups:**

- What belief formed that day? What lie did you start believing?

- Who did you have to become to survive that moment?
