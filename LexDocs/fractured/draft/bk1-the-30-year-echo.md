# The 30-Year Echo

## What age ten did to a man in his forties

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/a278108b-c4d0-448f-86c0-c81277e7d037-RackMultipart20251130-141-5zps2m.png)

Hospital lights flatten faces. That’s the first thing I remember clearly.

Three days after the wreck, ceiling tiles became my schedule. Count them between nurses. Count them between pain spikes. A chaplain tried to pray. I watched his lips move and felt nothing but the edge of the bed and the beep of machines.

Nobody gave me a playbook, so my nervous system wrote its own:

- Pain goes private.

- Worth = output.

- Vulnerability = risk.

As a kid, that code probably kept me sane. As a founder, it paid. But there’s interest on those old loans. It shows up 30 years later in boardrooms, budgets, and kitchens at midnight.

### When performance becomes oxygen

As a kid, “doing” was my way of earning permission to exist.

- Help more.

- Complain less.

- Make it easier on everyone else.

You don’t consciously decide this at ten. It just becomes obvious: the adults are overloaded, the pain is confusing, the one thing I control is my output.

Fast forward three decades:

- Exits.

- Titles.

- People calling me “driven” like it’s a compliment and a warning at the same time.

What I didn’t see: I had quietly installed a **performance-for-safety exchange** everywhere:

- With investors: “If I hit impossible timelines, you’ll stay.”

- With teams: “If I carry the weight, you won’t leave.”

- With family: “If I provide, I don’t have to feel.”

It works. Until it doesn’t.

### The boardroom where the echo showed up

Three decades later, I’m in a boardroom. We’re behind plan. Investor leans forward:

> “We need a hero date. Something big. Something we can rally the Street and the team around.”

I felt the echo hit like a reflex:

- **Smile.**

- **Say yes.**

- **Bleed later.**

The ten-year-old inside knew this drill:

- Mature adults are tense.

- They want impossible things.

- You make it okay by carrying more than you should.

But that day, I heard it _as_ an echo, not a command.

I kept my mouth shut. Let the silence sit there for a beat longer than was comfortable.

Then:

> “No hero dates,” I said. “Here’s the buffered plan and the fuse we’ll pull if we slip.”

New code running on old hardware.

- No adrenaline timeline.

- Real contingencies.

- No future version of me that has to eat glass to make this slide true.

The deal still happened. The world didn’t end. The ten-year-old sulked for a minute then went quiet.

### The echo has four voices

The old operating system doesn’t disappear. It just changes costumes. For me, it talks in four voices:

1. **Speed = safety**

2. **Charm = safety**

3. **Pain = proof**

4. **Alone = control**

The echo gave me some impressive muscles:

- Stamina.

- Calm under fire.

- Appetite for hard problems.

It also taxed the joints:

- Emotional distance at home.

- Brittle project timelines.

- Mis-hiring charismatic under-performers.

- Treating burnout as proof of commitment instead of a warning.

### Rewriting the code with patches, not a full reinstall

I didn’t wipe everything and become a new person. That’s not real. I added **patches**.

#### 1. Speed → Cadence

Instead of “move fast or die,” we run on **fixed cadences** :

- Weekly builder call: no pitch, no theater. Just “what held, what failed, what we’re actually doing next.”

- Monthly “red team” review: people I trust trying to break the plan on purpose.

- Quarterly “no launch” week: fix skeins, clean debt, no new promises.

Cadence beats panic.

#### 2. Charm → Checks

Instead of letting charisma override reality:

- Three **blind references** minimum for key hires.

- 90-day **scorecards** written _before_ equity or big titles.

- “Would I trust this person in a storm?” as a hard filter.

Charm becomes data, not a green light.

#### 3. Pain → Policy

Instead of treating scars as shame or superstition, we made them **policy** :

- Scar Map on paper.

- Budget fuses: lines where we automatically slow/halt segments before I “hero” it.

- Role fuses: nobody can sit in a role where every week is an emergency.

Pain becomes a spec, not a religion.

#### 4. Alone → Load Path

Instead of “I’ll carry it,” we draw the path:

> **Market → Investors/Partners → Me → Execs → Team → Home**

Panic doesn’t go downstream.

- Teams get **clarity** , not chaos.

- Home gets **signal** , not emotional runoff.

If I’m too fried to protect that path, that’s a fuse. Something has to de-risk upstream.

### Where it shows up at home

The echo doesn’t just whisper in boardrooms. It shows up on Tuesday nights.

- Speed = “Let me just finish this one thing.” Dinner gets cold.

- Charm = deflecting hard conversations with jokes.

- Pain = manufacturing drama when things are calm.

- Alone = locking myself in the office instead of saying “I’m overwhelmed.”

Patches at home look like:

- Hard stop times on the calendar.

- “Last call” alarm at 9:30 p.m. If I see a laptop after that, I ask why.

- A rule with my wife: if she says, “Are you here or somewhere else?” I answer honestly, no defensiveness.

The kid who survived by performance deserves respect. He does not get to run my marriage.

### Leadership is deciding who gets the keys

That’s the real shift.

The ten-year-old version of me:

- Thinks everything is urgent.

- Believes pain is the same thing as meaning.

- Thinks speed and charm can fix anything.

The forty-something version:

- Knows some deadlines are made up.

- Values margins, not martyrdom.

- Understands that clarity and proximity beat heroics.

The kid earned us a bridge to here. He gets a seat in the car. He doesn’t get the keys.

### Cutter Rule

**Respect the kid who got you here. Don’t let him run the company.**
