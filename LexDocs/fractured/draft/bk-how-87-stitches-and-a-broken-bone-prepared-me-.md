# BK: How 87 Stitches and a Broken Bone Prepared Me for 40 Surgeries and a Comeback

From a young age, I lived life on the edge. Growing up on a farm in rural Ohio, pushing limits was in my DNA. It wasn't just about the thrill; it was about the challenge, the adrenaline, and the sheer joy of testing boundaries. I learned early on that sometimes, those limits pushed back hard, leaving me with scars—both visible and hidden—that would shape my resilience and determination in the years to come.

By the time I turned ten, I had already amassed 87 stitches and broken a bone. Each injury came with its own story, its own lesson in pain and recovery. There was the time I fell from the barn loft, trying to impress my friends with a daring jump. The resulting gash on my leg needed twenty stitches. Then there was the incident with the barbed wire fence, where a miscalculated climb left my arm shredded and in need of another dozen stitches. Each of these moments taught me something about my body, my limits, and my ability to heal.

One of the most significant injuries before the accident that would change my life forever was a broken arm. It happened during a wrestling match at school. I was determined to win, pushing myself harder than ever. But one wrong move, one slip, and I found myself on the mat, my arm bent at an unnatural angle. The pain was excruciating, but I refused to let it break my spirit. I got back up, finished the match, and then went to the hospital. It was a small victory, but it showed me that I could endure more than I thought possible.

These experiences, as painful as they were, laid the foundation for what was to come. They taught me how to cope with physical pain, how to push through it, and most importantly, how to get back up after being knocked down. Little did I know, these lessons would be crucial in facing the greatest challenge of my life.

On October 29, 1990, my world was turned upside down. I was ten years old, riding an ATV, when I pulled out in front of a car. The impact was brutal. I was thrown onto the road, my helmet shattered, and my left foot was nearly torn off. I screamed for help, the pain overwhelming, as blood poured from my injuries. The image of that day is forever etched in my memory—the sight of my foot, mangled and barely attached, the sound of my own screams, the feeling of utter helplessness.

The accident left me bedridden and handicapped, with a long road of recovery ahead. Over the next decade, I would endure 32 surgeries, with an additional eight later on, bringing the total to 40. Each surgery was a battle, a test of my resilience and determination. The doctors had to reconstruct my foot, taking a muscle flap from my back and using skin grafts from various parts of my body to close the wounds. It was a painful and grueling process, one that required immense mental and physical strength.

I spent months in the hospital, isolated from my friends and family, with only my thoughts to keep me company. It was during these long, lonely days that I truly understood the importance of resilience. I had to learn how to walk again, over and over, as my leg muscles atrophied and my foot required more surgeries. Each time, it felt like starting from scratch. The frustration was immense, but I refused to give up.

The pain was a constant companion, but it was also a reminder of what I had already endured. Those 87 stitches and the broken bone from my earlier years had prepared me for this. They had taught me that pain is temporary, that healing is possible, and that I had the strength to overcome even the most challenging obstacles.

As I lay in my hospital bed, I would often think back to those earlier injuries. I remembered the determination it took to get back up, the grit required to push through the pain. I used those memories as fuel, motivating myself to keep going, to keep fighting. I knew that if I could overcome those challenges, I could overcome this too.

The recovery process was long and arduous. There were times when I felt like giving up, when the pain and frustration seemed too much to bear. But I had learned from my past that giving up was never an option. I pushed through each setback, each surgery, with the same determination that had gotten me through those earlier injuries.

By the time I reached my freshman year of college, I had gone through countless cycles of skin grafts, crutches, and learning to walk again. Each cycle was a new beginning, a fresh start. It was as if I was constantly being reborn, each time a little stronger, a little more resilient. Those early injuries had given me the tools I needed to face this challenge head-on, to rebuild myself time and time again.

Looking back, I realize that those 87 stitches and the broken bone were not just physical scars; they were badges of honor, symbols of my resilience and determination. They were the training ground for the greatest battle of my life, preparing me for the endless surgeries and the long road to recovery. Each scar, each stitch, was a testament to my ability to endure, to heal, and to come back stronger.

In the end, it wasn't just the surgeries that defined me; it was the journey, the countless times I got back up after being knocked down. It was the resilience I built, the grit I developed, and the unwavering determination to keep moving forward. Those early injuries, as painful as they were, gave me the strength to face the challenges ahead and emerge victorious.

Today, I carry those lessons with me in everything I do. Whether in my career, my personal life, or any new challenge I face, I know that I have the strength to overcome. I have been tested, time and time again, and I have proven to myself that I can endure, I can heal, and I can come back stronger. It is a lesson I carry with me always, a reminder that no matter how tough the road may be, I have the resilience to keep going.
