# Therapy, Loneliness, and Clarity

## When healing makes the distance louder

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/9c390b0e-ca72-4c07-a95f-c58aaf51d808-RackMultipart20251130-171-uqp0of.png)

Therapy didn’t erase the ache. It gave it a name. It didn’t make me less intense. It just made the intensity less radioactive for the people who share my house.

Before that, everything lived in one bucket:

- **Lonely = bad**

- **Busy = safe**

If I felt off, I sprinted. Spin up another company, another deck, another meeting. Call it “momentum,” call it “grind.” Same pattern: I was regulating pain with velocity and pretending it was strategy.

### The question that broke it open

“Where do you go when you’re tired?” “Into my head.” “Why?” “It’s quiet in there. And I win.”

In the room, it sounded clever. On the drive home, it felt like a confession.

I learned to disappear at ten. Hospital, wreck, chaos—whatever the exact origin story is, the pattern is clear:

- Pain went private.

- Performance paid.

I built an entire life on that operating system.

- In school: straight-A’s bought me space.

- In work: output bought me safety.

- In relationships: “being useful” substituted for “being present.”

It paid. I got titles, exits, a network, options. It also left me in a garage at 11:30 p.m., cheering alone at some half-finished project while my family watched TV without me.

That’s the part therapy turned the volume up on.

### The sentence that changed the room

My wife finally said the quiet part at 12:40 a.m. in the kitchen, leaning on the counter, no drama.

> “We need you, not the person who’s always almost done.”

No edge. No performance. Just truth laid flat.

I wanted to argue. “This _is_ for us. The companies, the land, the future, the…” But I heard it: I was _always_ almost done. Almost done with the next raise. Almost done with the next deck. Almost done with the next “just one more…”.

My presence was perpetually deferred. She was being asked to live with a future version of me that never arrived.

### Two kinds of loneliness

Therapy didn’t fix me. It sorted me.

First, we separated **two kinds of lonely** that used to feel identical:

- **Lonely / unsafe** Old panic wearing a modern hoodie. Signs:

- **Lonely / clean** Chosen solitude. No drama, just distance. Signs:

I stopped treating every ache like an emergency. Some loneliness was just the cost of being a builder. Some was a red light.

### The noon check-in

We built a stupidly simple system:

> **At noon, I check the column.**

I literally ask:

> “Is this lonely/unsafe or lonely/clean?”

If it’s **unsafe** , I don’t negotiate with it. I change my state in ten minutes or less:

- Water (full glass, not a sip).

- Walk (around the block, no phone).

- Push-ups (fail a set, reset my nervous system).

- Call one of three people who understand “builder-mode” and don’t need context.

If it’s **clean** , I don’t try to scrub it out. I let the ache ride shotgun and keep building. I don’t chase noise. I don’t invent fake urgency. I just notice it and move.

That one habit did more than any productivity hack I’ve touched.

### Boundaries as contracts, not vibes

The second thing we fixed was the resentment loop at home.

I used to run this script:

- Take on a brutal season at work.

- Tell myself “It’ll calm down soon.”

- Not actually tell anyone else that.

- Get resentful when they “didn’t understand how much I was doing.”

Of course they didn’t. I hadn’t told them the rules.

Now I frame it like this:

> “I’m going heads-down for three months. You get one hour a day and one day a week that are fully yours. After that, I disappear. I love you. I’m choosing this. It has a finish line.”

Not romance. A contract.

Do I hit it perfectly? No. But something radical happened:

- **Resentment dropped when ambiguity did.** They could see the shape of the season. They got to agree or push back. I wasn’t silently moving goalposts and then feeling unappreciated.

### Stop recruiting your family as regulators

The ugliest thing therapy showed me: I was using the people I loved as **regulation tools**.

- “Quick update” during dinner → I call it “sharing my passion.” What I’m really doing is chasing validation.

- Picking fights when I’m overclocked → not because I’m mad at them, but because conflict feels more real than numbness.

- Oversharing stress → not for advice, but to offload anxiety.

So I built an alternative:

1. **Morning paper dump**

2. **If the weight is still there at 2 p.m., I earn a call.**

3. **Everyone else gets lighthouse updates, not floodlights.**

They know I’m alive, moving, and still part of the human race. They aren’t being used as my emotional sump pump.

### Maintenance beats miracles

I kept the therapist monthly. Not because I’m broken or special. Because maintenance beats miracles.

We patch things before they blow:

- If the noon check is “unsafe” three days in a row, that’s the agenda.

- If Bridge Hour has turned into “Bridge 12 Minutes,” we talk about why.

- If I start fantasizing about burning everything down or starting three new ventures, we treat that as a signal, not a heroic impulse.

The ache didn’t leave. It got honest.

Now I can sit in **clean loneliness** without:

- Rage-scrolling.

- Picking a fight.

- Spinning up useless work.

I don’t dump it on my people. I don’t use them to regulate my pain. I build systems that do.

### A concrete test: proximity over performance

A Saturday in the garage told me the healing was real.

Old me:

- Headphones on.

- Car project spread across every flat surface.

- Son walks in → “Give me 10 minutes, bud.” Thirty minutes later he’s gone. I’m still under the truck.

New me: He walks in holding a frog he found at the creek. I put the wrench down. We sit on the concrete like it’s a board meeting. He explains frog logistics with full seriousness. I don’t think about my inbox once.

I felt it in my chest:

- **Proximity over performance.**

- Same standards. Human pace.

That’s the experiment: can I keep winning in the world without losing the room I live in?

### Cutter Rule

**Don’t use your people to regulate your pain. Build systems that do.**
