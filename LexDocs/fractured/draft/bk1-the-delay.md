# The Delay

## The villain of my story isn't the lady who hit me. It's time.

I eat fast. I drive fast when I get the opportunity. I save clicks. I save steps. I will walk a path once and then reroute to shave off three seconds the next time I walk it, and I will do this without thinking, the way other people breathe.

People who know me think I'm impatient. People who work with me think I'm intense. People who love me think I'm wired wrong. And maybe they're all right. But the thing none of them know — the thing I didn't know until I sat with it long enough to see it — is where it comes from.

It comes from a couple of hours in Wooster, Ohio.

---

When the car hit me, I was ten. I woke up on the road and the world was wrong. The details of the impact I've told before and I'll tell again — the eyes connecting through the windshield, the nothing after, the waking up. But the part I haven't told is what happened between the road and the helicopter.

They took me to Wooster Community Hospital. It was the closest one. And for a couple of hours, they held me there while they figured out what they were going to do.

A couple of hours.

That doesn't sound like much. You wait a couple of hours at the DMV. You wait a couple of hours for a flight delay. A couple of hours is nothing. Unless you're a ten-year-old with a destroyed foot and the clock is running on whether the tissue lives or dies.

In those hours, infection set in. By the time they realized this was bigger than Wooster — by the time they called the life flight from Akron — the damage had compounded. The flesh that could have been saved was dying. The skin that could have been grafted was turning. The foot that needed two or three surgeries was now going to need twenty.

Seconds, minutes, hours. They matter.

---

The life flight was fifteen minutes. I remember it happening fast. I must have passed out and slept most of it, because I don't remember the air or the altitude or any of the things you'd think a kid would remember about his first helicopter ride. What I remember is getting wheeled out and the noise — the jet engine — this deafening mechanical scream that was louder than anything I'd ever heard, louder than the accident, louder than my own fear.

And then I was in Akron. And then I was out for two days.

When I woke up in the ICU, I thought it was a dream. I closed my eyes and went back to sleep and woke up and I was right back where I was. And I knew not much time had gone by. And I thought: this is real.

My parents weren't there. They'd been sent home because I'd been asleep for so long. They were an hour away. And I was alone with the beeps and the ceiling tiles and the understanding — not the words for it yet, just the feeling — that time had done something to me that the car had only started.

---

Here's what the Wooster delay cost me.

The infection meant gangrene. The gangrene meant they had to cut away everything that was dying — all the skin, all the flesh, down to where you could see the ligaments. The cutting meant reconstruction. The reconstruction meant a muscle flap — the latissimus dorsi out of my back, skin from my right leg, laid over the top of my foot like you're rebuilding a house after the foundation cracked. And then the skin grafts. Full thickness. Partial thickness. Half a dozen sourced patches from my right leg alone. Little scars all over, like a map of everywhere they took from to give back to the foot.

And then the grafts didn't take. And they did it again. And then they didn't take again. And they did it again.

Forty surgeries. Nine months of my childhood on crutches. Nine months in my dad's pickup truck driving to and from doctors' appointments. Every time you come home, you go back up in two days. Every time the graft takes, you hope. Every time it doesn't, you learn how to get back up.

I have a scar on my back from the muscle flap that curves at an angle. I tell people it's a shark bite. They believe me until they don't. The real story is worse than a shark and less interesting at parties.

I got a million dollar foot.

And it started with a couple of hours in a waiting room in Wooster.

---

I didn't understand this for thirty-five years. I just thought I was fast. I thought I was efficient. I thought I was wired for speed the way some people are wired for music or numbers — something you're born with, something you don't question.

But you're not born hating wasted time. You learn it.

I learned it in a hospital where hours turned to gangrene. I learned it on an operating table where minutes determined whether a graft would take or fail. I learned it on crutches where every step was a negotiation between pain and progress and the ticking awareness that this step could be the one that tears it open again.

When I was a kid, I had a model SR-71 Blackbird on my headboard. Fastest plane ever built. And a Ferrari Testarossa next to it. And I raced four-wheelers and ATVs and wanted to win at everything, because races are how you prove you're faster than the thing that's chasing you.

I didn't know what was chasing me. I just knew I couldn't stop.

My dad had 175 laps at the Michigan International Speedway through Track Time. I've done fifty. He drove Chevrolets and hated Fords and the truck was always moving, always heading to the next well, the next site, the next thing that needed doing. He didn't waste time either. But his came from somewhere else — from building a business with no degree, from drilling three hundred wells with handshake deals and a reputation. His speed was earned. Mine was defensive.

---

Today I walk on my toes. Permanent high heels, essentially. My foot hurts every single day of my life. I have appreciation for people with foot pain because I live it — the constant negotiation between standing and not standing, between pushing through and paying for it later.

And I still save seconds. I still reroute the path. I still eat fast and drive fast and optimize clicks and shave steps and get there quicker with the same result.

Not because I'm impatient. Because a ten-year-old learned that time is not neutral. Time is not just passing. Time is doing something — building or destroying, healing or infecting, saving or costing. And if you waste it, you pay for it in flesh.

Forty surgeries taught me how to get back up. But the Wooster delay taught me something more dangerous: that the getting-back-up wouldn't have been necessary if someone had moved faster.

So I move faster. At everything. Always. Not because I want to. Because I learned what happens when you don't.

---

People ask me if I ever think about the Shawn who didn't get hit. The alternate version. The kid who made it across the road and picked up the gasoline and rode back up the driveway and went to the pizza party the next day and grew up to be the fastest kid in school and maybe played football and maybe didn't need forty surgeries and maybe didn't learn to convince himself that he liked pain.

I don't spend much time on that Shawn.

There's infinite regression in that direction. You don't actually get anywhere. You're just running alternate timelines in your head, and every one of them branches into a thousand more, and none of them are real, and none of them help.

I'd rather be mapping out all of the potentials of the future. So I can better predict it. So I can move through it faster. So I can make the seconds count.

Because I know what they cost when you don't.
