# **BK:** Prologue: A Life in Perspective

**Opening Reflection:**

In the stillness of early dawn, amidst the familiar backdrop of the farm where my story began, or in the quiet of my office where it unfolds, I often ponder a bittersweet truth: "What doesn’t break you will make you stronger." Yet, life's trials often do more than challenge; they can shatter, leaving visible and hidden scars. The first two decades of my life were a crucible, teaching me that I was indeed the axis of my world. The subsequent years were an odyssey of harnessing a creative genius born from chaos and survival.

Dreaming of NFL stardom like Joe Montana, or military feats akin to Rambo, I was a rebel at heart, growing up in the rugged terrain of rural Ohio, amidst a backdrop of Amish simplicity. But my world irrevocably changed on an autumn day in 1990, at ten years old. An ATV accident nearly ended my life, leaving me handicapped and leading to an endless series of surgeries — 40 in total. Each scar, each stitch, a grim testament to a battle-hardened youth.

**Early Roots and Core Values:**

My father, a fusion of roughneck tenacity and farming resilience, set the stage for a childhood steeped in harsh lessons and raw experiences. The farm was both a sanctuary and a battleground, where the values of grit and perseverance were ingrained in me. In this dichotomy of life – amidst the nurturing fields and the unforgiving oil rigs – I learned the true cost of hard work and ambition.

**Turning Points and Transformation:**

The aftermath of the accident was a descent into a labyrinth of pain and self-reflection. In the solitude of hospital rooms and recovery beds, I confronted the harshest realities of life. This period was a crucible of transformation — renouncing long-held beliefs, questioning the essence of faith and fate. My fascination with technology was kindled in these depths, a beacon in the darkness. The family computer became my escape, my arena for rebuilding a shattered identity.

**Journey of Resilience and Innovation:**

The resilience I cultivated in those dark times became the bedrock for my entrepreneurial ventures. I rose from the ashes of physical and emotional turmoil, channeling my experiences into creative power. This journey led me to establish CV Labs, and later, agMD and EnergiAcres, endeavors that culminate a life lived at the intersection of adversity and innovation. These ventures are not just business to me; they are personal crusades to redefine industries that once defined me.

**Philosophical Musings:**

I've learned that life's clock can reset in a heartbeat through personal loss or global shifts like climate change. Each setback and rebirth teaches us to walk anew, to reforge our paths. EnergiAcres and agMD are more than just companies; they are manifestations of my journey — a commitment to revolutionize agriculture and energy, guided by lessons learned in the harshest classrooms of life.

**Connecting with the Reader:**

As you embark on this journey through the pages of my life, be prepared for a story that delves into the shadows as much as it reaches for the light. This is not just an account of overcoming odds; it’s a raw, unvarnished tale of survival, transformation, and the relentless pursuit of a vision. Welcome to my world — a world where every scar tells a story, and every chapter is a step towards redefining what's possible. This is not just a story of becoming a 'learned genius'; it's a narrative of transformation, where resilience, grit, and a relentless drive for improvement are at the heart of every chapter.

## The letter I wrote my Dad after his passing

I was trying to figure out whether losing someone suddenly or over time is better. But, I don't think that question needs an answer. Ultimately, I have lost someone who meant so much, and that cannot be changed. Someone I cared about deeply, who had a hard time showing his feelings, but I know he loved me more than anything. I want to share some memories of my dad to illustrate the kind of father he was and what he meant to me, my brothers and sister, and everyone here.

My relationship with my dad was turbulent at times, a sentiment many here today can relate to. From early on, I learned that hard work is required to make something of yourself. Nothing comes free or easy, but you can achieve it with effort. If you were going to do something, you were in it to win it, or as he put it, "kick ass and take names." In second grade, at my first major wrestling tournament, the Ashland JAWS tournament, we were there to win. If my brother CJ and I took first place, we would see Genesis in Cleveland on their "I Can't Dance" tour. We didn't know then, but he had already got the tickets. We were going regardless, but it sweetened the victory when I beat my longtime rival, and future friend, Chad Wright. It wasn’t always easy, though; losing was the worst, and you better not cry.

October 29th marked the 20th anniversary of an event that changed my life forever: getting hit by a car. Carina and I were discussing the events that followed the accident. I called my parents to understand their perspective. Learning this information now, as a father myself, holds new meaning. My mom recalled how the EMTs, nurses, and doctors in the ER wouldn't let her see me due to the severity of my injuries. I remember lying on the table, staring into lights, when suddenly my dad appeared, pushing past the nurses in his black and gold Cutter Oil coat and hat. Despite warnings, his determination to see me showed his deep care; he wouldn’t let anyone stop him from seeing his son. I don't recall his exact words, but I remember his strong presence, ensuring I didn't see any fear he undoubtedly felt. Twenty years later, this new information made me feel good about who my dad was.

I spent a long time in the hospital, and rooms didn't have telephones back then. My dad ensured I had his cell phone, which you had to carry in a bag so I could use it if needed. I also spent much time riding in the truck with him as he checked wells and took me to all my doctor’s appointments. Initially, I was in a wheelchair, then crutches. Eventually, I was able to hop tanks on one foot. I hated 210’s. The worst was forgetting the number between the tank and the truck. My dad would get annoyed, a feeling I understood, as he wouldn't forget such things. After the accident, I underwent at least two surgeries a year and countless doctor appointments, most of which my dad took me to. My accident forever altered my life; physical work wasn't in the cards, although I managed to be a farm manager for some years. He advised me to use my intellect and gift and not to work as hard as he had to. That's what I'm doing now.

My dad and I didn't get along well during my teenage years. Many teenagers experience this, but for me, it was exacerbated by my rebellion against authority. I got into trouble and threw several parties at the house but could never escape farm chores. I learned from my dad that you can party, but you must be ready for work. An instance where this lesson played in my favor was on senior skip day. The night before, we partied at Pee Wee Hollow. Knowing I was off school, my dad made me work. The following Monday, Dr. Frame, our principal, tried to catch those who skipped. He thought he had me, claiming a witness saw me at Sterling Farm Equipment that Friday. However, I had the perfect alibi - working for my dad. Shawn 1, Dr. Frame 0.

My dad and I were at odds through most of college. At some point, things changed, though I can't pinpoint when. There was growth on my part and acceptance on his. I realized he had raised me right, and I was turning out OK. I also had to accept that my dad wouldn't always be perfect. He was my dad, and I was lucky to have him. At some point, I began talking to him more than my mom, sometimes hanging up the phone in disbelief at our conversations. We still argued, but our relationship was one we both cherished. He supported me and offered sound advice when I started my business. He was the first to fly with me when I got my pilot’s license, an experience neither my mom nor wife has shared. I'll always cherish the times we spent together, especially family vacations. These moments have taught me the value of time over material possessions. My brother Josh put it well when he said he didn't want presents anymore, just family vacations. I wish we could have had a few more.

We went to the condo on a Saturday a month and a half ago. I planned to ride the jet ski, return for dinner, and pick up my parents from the airport the next day. The jet ski broke down in a remote area, leading to a two-hour ordeal pulling it through neck-deep water. When I finally returned, the first person I called was my dad. He asked about the jet ski, what went wrong, and why I didn't take my cell phone. He joked about the situation, saying, "Well, you were calling me, so I knew you were okay." He would have mobilized a search party for me if I had been genuinely stuck. When you needed him, he was there.

We never got to say goodbye or share these feelings. I hope he knew how much he meant to me and all of us. It's tough to move on, but we will make it; we are Cutters. He left too soon, which hurts, but he prepared us well. If we mess up, we won’t hear it from him. I would welcome his guidance now.

He taught us a lot, but the most important lesson was his last: Work hard, play hard, and spend time with family. Even if you don't always get along, make it work. Find the time. Leave some work for tomorrow. Because if tomorrow never comes, the work doesn’t matter. All you have is today. Make me proud.

Your son, Shawn

There is an overused saying “What doesn’t break you, will make you stronger” that is only part of the story because sometimes life does break you, and recovery can take a lifetime. During the first twenty years of my life, I learned that I am the center of my own universe. I did not start down the learned genius path. My original plan had me going to the NFL to play quarterback, just like Joe Montana. I was the kid who wore 49ers jerseys in a Cleveland Browns family because I aligned with the best. I grew up in rural Ohio, Amish country about 45 minutes south of Cleveland. And if, by chance, the Pro Football career did not work out, the military, especially the Special Forces, just like Rambo, was plan B. I was certain of one thing, I was destined for greatness.

At age ten, on October 29th, 1990, everything in my life would take a hard left turn when I pulled out in front of a car while riding an ATV. Spoiler alert, I was messed up, nearly died, and got a ride on a helicopter, permanently handicapped, and 32 surgeries later I am writing this book. There have been eight other surgeries, bringing the total to 40 even, more on those later. As I look back, the overwhelming theme in my life is having to get back up.

The accident changed me in ways that I am still trying to sort out in my head; this book may be one of the tools to unravel some of my mysteries. I was accident-prone, with a few broken bones and 87 stitches before the car accident. My Mom was concerned that Child Protective Services would start asking questions. Getting stitches and having a scar became a badge of honor for me; scars showed everyone how tough I was. The accident also landed me with my Dad for an estimated nine months of my life, riding copilot with him because he took me to most of my Doctor’s appointments since the oil and gas wells he operated were in the vicinity of Akron Children’s Hospital.

I’ve observed only a small fraction of the population that ever faced the horrific experience I did that day. A fraction of a second I was in the wrong place at the wrong time and it meant that every day from that day would be different. The accident put me in a bed and wheelchair for months and crutches for years. weeks alone in the hospital causes any person to look inward at one point or another. I had enough time in bed to deconstruct the entire scene leading up to the accident and start the quest for the meaning of life. I went frame by frame to identify the part of my day that went wrong and put me in that spot on the road, and in the end, it was chance, dumb luck. I would not call my family religious, but we did attend church on important holidays. I remember renouncing God in the hospital bed and abstaining from Church events wherever possible after that. The Judeo-Christian God had too many contradictions for my taste.

The recovery time was also time for me to read, learn, and dive head-first into computers. The family oil and gas company purchased an i386 computer to perform the company's revenue accounting functions, and I used the computer whenever my Mom was not using it. I learned how to build my first game on that computer and how royalty and working interest were calculated for oil and gas units.

My journey to understand the world started first with everything external but would eventually find a way inside via brain entrainment and meditation, thanks to a mudlogger named Walt who I met while onsite in the late 90’s. Walt would also give me a dozen book recommendations that would change my worldly view forward.

What we think, we become. If there is one thing you take from this book, it’s just that. Simple, like Descartes once said, I think, therefore, I am. The accident has also shown me how to care for others because so many people around me cared for me when I was in need.
