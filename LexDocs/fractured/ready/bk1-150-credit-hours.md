# 150 Credit Hours

## I graduated with 150 credit hours, which was stupid. But I was there to learn.

Here is what 180 pounds of Shawn Cutter looked like at Miami University in 1999: a kid who worked out intensely, who wanted to see how big he could get, how strong he could get, and — let's be honest — how many girls he could get going to Miami. That was on my mind. I was not above admitting it then and I'm not above admitting it now.

I was a meathead. I was also secretly a nerd. The problem was that I didn't know I was a nerd yet, and I was actively revolting against the possibility.

I'd started at the University of Akron on a full academic scholarship. Which means someone, somewhere, had looked at my test scores and my transcript and decided that my brain was my ticket. I should have taken that as a signal. Instead I took it as an obligation I wasn't sure I wanted.

Akron had chemistry. I fucking hated chemistry. It was a weed-out class, and I knew — with the specific certainty of someone who has already been weeded out of enough things in his life — that I was not going to take three more years of chemistry classes. It wasn't me. The honors program wasn't me either. I didn't fit in. Wrong crowd, wrong pace, something about the shape of it that didn't match the shape of me. I thought: let's not repeat that mistake.

But Akron had philosophy. And philosophy opened something up.

What it opened was how *expansive* knowledge is. Where it started. Why it started. What we were trying to explain — not just the answers but the questions underneath the answers, the questions that generated the fields, the questions that people had been asking for twenty-five hundred years and still hadn't closed. That was interesting to me. That was the kind of thing my brain wanted to chase.

I had the last surgery — a skin graft on my foot — and then I transferred to Miami. New start. I decided not to join the honors program because the Akron version had taught me that whatever I was, I wasn't that. But I also wasn't just a meathead. I was something in between, and Miami let me be in between.

I took everything. Philosophy. Film studies. World religions. Not because they were required — because I was interested. Because once philosophy had cracked the door on how expansive knowledge was, I wanted to walk through every other door in the hallway. What did film know that philosophy didn't? What did world religions understand about human motivation that neither film nor philosophy could fully explain? What happened when you stacked these things?

You graduated with 150 credit hours. That's what happened.

The standard is around 120. I overshot by 30 credits, which is roughly an extra year of coursework that I took because I wanted to, because the curiosity was bigger than the degree requirements. My advisor probably thought I was inefficient. I think I was doing the thing I was actually there to do.

There is a version of me that goes to Akron, stays in the honors program, takes the chemistry, becomes whatever that pipeline was designed to produce. That version exists somewhere in a parallel timeline. He probably has a fine career. He probably doesn't start a company in a garage.

The version that actually happened — the one who transferred after a skin graft, who took philosophy and film and world religions and 150 credit hours of whatever looked interesting, who was simultaneously a meathead and a closet intellectual, who worked out like an animal and then went home and thought about epistemology — that version was assembling a toolkit he didn't know he'd need.

Systems thinking. First principles. The ability to sit with an unanswered question. The instinct to look at a field and see not just what's there but the structure underneath it. All of that started in those classrooms, those electives, those extra 30 credit hours that nobody asked me to take.

The meathead is long gone. The nerd won. But I'm glad they coexisted for a while. The meathead gave me the body to survive. The nerd gave me everything else.

**Cutter Rule:** Take the elective. Take the class nobody told you to take. The credit hours that don't count toward the degree are the ones that count toward the person.
