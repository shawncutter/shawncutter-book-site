# Private Infra First: Building Without Grants

## If funding won’t meet us, we build around the gap.

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/22d792fc-353b-4d52-80bc-961d57575e0e-RackMultipart20251130-150-a2ftqj.png)

Scene: It’s barely light out in Mansfield. Coffee in one hand, Sharpie in the other, you’re bent over a rolled-up plan set on the tailgate. Three alternate tap paths in red. Two substation options in blue. Not a single grant logo anywhere on the page.

This is the pivot point for me:

I spent years trying to thread the grant needle—federal alphabet soup, state programs, “innovation” pots, regional councils, volunteer steering committees. Smart people, good intentions, but the system itself is slow, brittle, and misaligned with what it takes to actually pour concrete in a specific field next to a specific town.

Grants taught me a lot. They also taught me what I _don’t_ want to depend on.

- Too many parties with no skin in the game.

- Timelines measured in election cycles, not construction seasons.

- Conditions written for generic “impact,” not the physics of a real site.

The net lesson: **public infrastructure first, without grants** —or more precisely, without _depending_ on them.

If we can make the campus pencil commercially, we get:

- Clear goals and alignment across the entire stack.

- Professional accountability instead of polite volunteer drift.

- Timelines measured in months and milestones, not “pending notice of award.”

If grants show up later, great. That’s a rebate, not a precondition.

### The stack we’re actually funding

Instead of chasing free money, we’re standardizing the blocks we know work and financing those like real infrastructure:

- **Gas taps and laterals** sized for both first tenant and long-term campus load.

- **Feeders and sectionalizers** designed to harden the local grid, not just serve us.

- **BESS (battery energy storage)** to buffer demand, firm renewables, and support neighbor circuits.

- **Microgrid controls** that can island critical loads and still play nice with the utility.

Partners get paid on **availability and performance** , not on how compelling our grant narrative sounds.

The dividend to the city is simple and measurable:

- **Reliability:** SAIDI/MAIFI down, not up.

- **Jobs:** real headcount in ops, maintenance, and buildout.

- **Timeline:** shovels based on engineering and contracts, not on a grant committee agenda.

I’m not anti-grant. I’m anti-fragility. When a project only works if a committee of people who don’t live there say yes, you don’t have a project—you have a wish.

### How we measure if it’s working

If you can’t measure it, you can’t defend it. So the KPI skeleton for “private infra first” looks like:

- **Days from pre-app → NTP (Notice to Proceed)**

- **Private dollars per kW of public benefit**

- **SAIDI/MAIFI delta**

- **Local vendor %**

These don’t fit in a ribbon-cutting speech, but they’re what actually matters.

### The discipline: fund physics, not press releases

The biggest temptation in this space is to treat announcements as progress.

- Press release about a grant _application_.

- Panel appearances.

- Renderings.

None of that powers a single pump or light bulb.

So we anchor on one simple rule:

> **Rule: Fund the physics, not the press release.**

If a dollar isn’t making electrons move more reliably, water flow more predictably, or steel show up on time, it’s probably overhead or theater.

### Mini Playbook: How to “go around the gap”
