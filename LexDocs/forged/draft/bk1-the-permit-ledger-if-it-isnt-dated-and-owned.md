# **The Permit Ledger** _If it isn’t dated and owned, it doesn’t exist._

Fluorescent buzz. Coffee ring on a one‑page grid. Three red cells glare like warning lights. Driveway permit. Feeder upgrade work order. Stormwater NOI. All “in progress,” none with a date. “Maybe” is where projects go to die.

I learned this in the oilfield after a week lost to a missing stamp. In the startup after a quarter slipped to “legal review.” On the farm when the auger sat idle because a $12 shear bolt wasn’t in the bin. The fix is boring: a ledger with owners and dates, published weekly. Silence triggers a 72‑hour escalation. No drama, just the next action.

We build the list out loud. City approvals: site plan, building, fire, noise. State: air permit (minor source). DOT: driveway cut and haul routes. Utility: interconnect studies, relay settings, recloser coordination. Environmental: SWPPP and BMPs. Each row gets four fields—owner, due date, blocker, link to drawing. We post the public slice every Friday by 3 p.m. Promise less, publish more.

The turn shows up fast. Monday, a red box. By Wednesday, the inbox has a stamped PDF and a question about a trench section. Thursday, a different red box—utility settings need a study addendum. Good. We can fix what we can see.

We don’t celebrate the ledger. We just run it. A one‑screen habit that keeps a mission from bleeding out through a thousand tiny cuts.

**AI tie.** AI’s job here is clerk and traffic cop. It parses submittals, extracts dates, suggests likely blockers, and drafts escalation emails with the right attachments. It flags age‑ing items and predicts slippage from lead‑time changes. It can’t charm a reviewer. It can make sure the right sheet is in the right inbox at the right hour. Logs before lipstick.

**Rule: Make the ledger public and the excuses vanish.**

**AI Playbook — Permit Ledger**  **Purpose:** Turn “maybe” into a dated queue you can manage. **Prompt:**

1. “From this permit checklist and drawing set, generate a single‑page RYG ledger with owner, due date, blocker, and document links.”

2. “Draft 72‑hour escalation templates for city/DOT/utility with a one‑paragraph status and next action.”

3. “Create a Friday 3 p.m. public update summary, excluding sensitive data, with age‑ing items called out.”

4. “Set reminders and produce a weekly diff (what turned green, what went red).” **Constraints:** One page • Screenshot‑clean • Links must resolve • 72‑hour SLA • No names in public slice, roles only.
