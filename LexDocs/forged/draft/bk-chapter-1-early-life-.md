### **Outdoor Adventures:** 

Growing up in the heart of rural Ohio, my heroes were not those whose feats were emblazoned in comic books; they were the rugged silhouettes of action stars like Rambo and Rocky and the everyday heroism of neighbors and family. Through the static of our fuzzy CBS reception, these figures leaped from screen to psyche, instilling in me the values of hard work and the resilience required to prevail against all odds.

I vividly remember the freedom that came with building forts—a task that was part playful construction, part earnest preparation. The wood was always scavenged with a strategic eye, solicited from the depths of my uncle's barn under the banner of patriotism. We were children of the Cold War era, after all, and defending our make-believe bastions from Soviet specters was a rite of childhood as much as it was a game.

My father, a complex figure himself, was my first hero and at times, my childhood terror. This dual nature led me to seek inspiration elsewhere, contributing to the mixed tapestry of role models that populated my youth. From blockbuster characters like Schwarzenegger's Commando to the real-life tales of my uncles and the local farmers, I learned about courage, cunning, and even empathy—key qualities that went into my making.

Our forts in the woods were epic, not just in their construction but in the escapist freedom they offered—a place where our parents' watchful eyes could not reach us, with half a mile of dense forest as our protective barrier. Our voices could carry across the valley to our cousins with whom we shared this unspoken bond of distant camaraderie, our shouts a celebration of the vastness that enveloped us.

While the rest of the world was transitioning into the digital age, our entertainment was largely of our own making. Limited to a handful of public air channels, our imaginations ran as wild as the landscape surrounding us. We staged elaborate battles, went on impromptu fishing expeditions to catch dinner in the neighbor's pond, and participated in those timeless childhood conspiracies that pitted us against an adult world we only partially understood.

As I look back on those days, I see them for the foundational experiences they were. They taught me a simple yet profound joy in creation, the value of resourcefulness, and the significant role imagination plays.

Now as a parent of children growing up in the city, reflect on the differences in lifestyle, values, and opportunities between your childhood and theirs.

Now, as a parent raising children in the city's pulse, I often find myself caught in a tug-of-war between nostalgia and reality. The lifestyles of my children are in stark contrast to my childhood—saturated with technology and structured activities, miles away from the sprawling, unfettered landscapes of rural Ohio. I grapple with the differences in values and opportunities and the challenge of imparting wisdom that is not eagerly solicited by modern youthful ears.

In the city, my children's days are filled with the melodies of ballet, the rhythm of dance, and the discipline of piano. The richness of these cultural offerings is something I marvel at, yet I can't help but feel a pang of loss for the raw, hands-on learning I experienced. I was a child of chores and dirt, with the freedom to explore and tinker; my education was as much among fields and animals as it was within the four walls of a school.

I see the wealth of enrichment experiences the city affords, yet I long to teach my children the appreciation of work, of getting their hands dirty, of understanding the way things come together and break apart. This intrinsic knowledge, a second nature to a farm-reared youth, feels like a vanishing currency in the urban sprawl.

Time feels like a relentless adversary. My father's teachings were seamlessly woven into the fabric of daily life—a life dictated by the land and seasons. In contrast, I find the integration of life lessons a more deliberate and, at times, a more contrived effort. I worry about the window of opportunity closing; will they grasp the knowledge I yearn to pass on before they're swept into the tide of adolescence?

Yet, it's not all solemn reflection. The city pulsates with enrichment and creative potential—an effervescent energy that could never be harnessed in the country. My children have opportunities at their fingertips that were beyond my wildest imaginations. It's a different kind of wild here—one that's lush with possibility and diverse experiences. The challenge lies not in the lack of opportunities but in helping them cultivate the same sense of self-reliance and resourcefulness that was my rite of passage.

My rural childhood, hewn from the very soil I toiled, has instilled in me a resilience and simplicity of joy. I carry the hope that through projects and shared experiences, I can kindle in my city-bred offspring a semblance of that earthy wisdom. I aim to teach them how to navigate the complexities of life, to be capable and competent—not just in the concrete jungle but beyond it, where the tapestry of nature weaves its quiet magic.

I wrestle with the irony that in the city, with its libraries, parks, and museums, it's somehow harder to teach a child about life's raw mechanics. The city whispers different lessons, ones of diversity, speed, and ceaseless innovation—valuable, yes, but different from the pastoral education of my youth.

As I watch my children, with their heads often turned by screens and schedules, I ache to infuse their days with a bit of the rustic grit from which I emerged. I want them to feel soil compact under their feet, to understand the cycles of growth and harvest, and to witness firsthand the silent stoicism of wildlife. These are the teachings I fear are slipping through the cracks of urbanity, yet they are the ones I hold most dear.

In my heart, I know that these efforts are more than just nostalgic whims; they are the keystones of the legacy I wish to leave to my children. To know the exhilaration of autonomy, the pride of creation, and the satisfaction of a hard day's work—these are gifts that I was given, ones that I must find new ways to pass on in the altered landscape of asphalt and ambition.

It is not the city life that I challenge, but rather, the form in which wisdom and character are imparted. My hope is to teach my children not only to navigate this urbanized world but also to draw from the deep well of rural knowledge, to imbue their lives with balance, and to appreciate the infinite tapestry that is our world. As my father did for me, I wish to root them in values and experiences that are timeless, that transcend the divide between the bucolic past and the metropolitan present.

A shear pin is a mechanical fastener used to prevent damage to machinery during a torque overload situation. It is designed to act as a sacrificial device to protect the machine's other, more expensive, or critical components from becoming damaged.

Here's how it works: the shear pin is made of a material with a known shear strength lower than the strength of the other parts in the assembly. It is inserted into a shaft or a coupling and aligned with another component. If the machinery experiences excessive force or loads because of a jam or overload, the shear pin breaks or shears off at a predetermined load, disconnecting the drive element from the driven element.

This breakage is a mechanical "fuse," stopping the torque transmission to prevent further damage. Once the jam is cleared or the overload removed, the broken shear pin can be replaced with a new one, and the machine can be put back into operation with minimal downtime and reduced repair costs.

### **Adventurous Tales:** 

The freedom of rural living brewed a kind of mischief and adventure that city streets could never contain. It was the unspoken permission to explore, to push boundaries, and to satiate our wild, youthful curiosities. Our expeditions were as limitless as the day was long, and every corner of the natural world seemed to whisper an invitation.

Fishing in the ponds was a rite as natural as breathing. No licenses or limits, simply the raw challenge between boy and bass, our poles arching over reflective waters as dragonflies skimmed the surface, witnesses to our patience and eventual triumph. The spoils were more than the catch; they were the stories and the scars from battles with mosquitos and deer flies, badges of honor we wore proudly.

Our ATVs were steeds of steel and rubber, carrying us on quests to find the best fishing spots or simply to carve new trails through unyielding foliage. Those trails weren't just tracks in the dirt; they were the product of our labor, marked by sweat and, occasionally, blood. We learned that the land would yield to determination, and a chainsaw's absence only meant our hands worked harder, guided by the ingenuity born of necessity.

The forts in the woods stood as castles of our conquest, epic in our eyes, though perhaps only ramshackle structures to an outsider. But to us, they were sanctuaries of sovereignty, complete with tripwires and booby traps to ward off imagined foes. Here, we fought our Cold War with fishing lines and alarm systems rigged for the impossible Russian incursion. The woods provided both canvas and cover, allowing us to vanish from parental oversight, each tree whispering secrets of independence and each leaf a page in our book of exploits.

Even our limited access to public airwaves couldn’t stifle our adventures. While three fuzzy channels tried to funnel the outside world to us, we were already out the door, living stories that no screen could capture. We were the children of the Nintendo era, yet our pixels were the stars overhead and our gamepads the dirt beneath our hands. Out here, our imaginations weren't fed; they were the source.

Yet, among these tales of wild exploits, there was always the undercurrent of the natural world’s lessons. Every fort built, every fish caught, and every trail blazed was a a masterclass in risk and reward. Nature was both our playground and our sternest teacher, instilling in us respect for the environment around us and an understanding of our place within it. We learned to read the signs of the coming storm, to navigate by the position of the sun, and to know the time of day by the chorus of cicadas.

As a young entrepreneur, I turned to the bounty of nature for my first business venture. I cultivated gourds and sold them on country acres, learning early on about the cycles of growth and commerce. These lessons in entrepreneurship were not found in textbooks but in the very soil, nurtured by my own hands, watered with the sweat of my brow, and brought to fruition under the wide-open sky.

I must admit the struggle in translating these values to my urban family life. Building community in the city has been a challenge that I have not yet mastered. I longed to recreate that camaraderie and mutual reliance that was ever-present in my youth. My neighbors and I shared harvests, swapped stories, and lent helping hands without a second thought. The city's rhythm differs—a cacophony of isolated beats rather than the harmonious melody of interconnected lives.

The nostalgia for my rural childhood remains potent, a constant undercurrent in my adult life. It is a reminder of the resilience and fortitude shaped by a life close to the land. It’s clear now that those youth adventures were not mere escapades; they were the building blocks of character, each day a new lesson in self-reliance and the infinite potential of imagination.

I realize I cannot replicate the past for my children, but I can instill in them the essence of those experiences. The value of a hard day's work, the pride of creation, and the joy found in the simplicity of nature are legacies I can pass on. As I balance the rustic wisdom of my rural upbringing with the realities of modern, urban life, I find solace in knowing that the adventures of my youth have prepared me for this very task. #### ice cube story

Before our life was bound to the rhythms of livestock and harvests, my childhood was rooted in the more modest farmlands outside West Salem, Ohio. There, amidst the sprawling fields of corn and soybeans, my father envisioned a sanctuary where his children would learn the virtues of hard work. And it was there, under the relentless Ohio sun, that a profound lesson was carved into my memory—a lesson initiated by something as innocuous as ice cubes.

I was five then, my sister a tender three, and CJ, the oldest among us, was seven. Our task was straightforward yet unyielding: to clear the fields of rocks and deposit them into the back of our truck, while our father continued planting. It was a sweltering June day when the incident occurred—over a disagreement that seems trivial in retrospect but was, perhaps, monumental to a child: CJ was consuming too many ice cubes.

To my father, this was not a mere act of thirst-quenching but an infraction worthy of fury. He perceived it as slightly against the labor we were meant to share equally. With wrath as scorching as the day, he disciplined CJ with a belt, his blows as strict as the lessons he intended to impart. It was then that I realized the weight of expectation upon us. Each action and decision bore consequences as tangible as the soil we tread upon.

That day etched in me a clear understanding of the standards my father held. It taught me the importance of contribution and sacrifice within our family unit—a microcosm of the more fantastic world's workings. As my brother bore the brunt of the punishment, resentment mixed with empathy in my young heart. Yet, the ethos of duty and the significance of each individual's role within a group were indelibly impressed upon me.

The "Ice Cube" incident, as it came to be known, was reflective of the ethos that governed our early years: a silent yet omnipresent testament to the fact that even the most minor actions can ripple through the fabric of a family, shaping character and defining relationships in ways that only time can fully reveal.

We did not have a farm with livestock until I was 10; before that, we farmed land outside of West Salem, Ohio. I remember my Dad growing corn and soybeans most of the time. This was where my Dad intended to teach his kids how to work, starting with picking up rocks.
