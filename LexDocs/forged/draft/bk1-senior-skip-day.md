# Senior Skip Day

## _Alibi math vs. the person in the mirror._

Pee Wee Hollow smelled like creek and leaf rot. The kind of place you hide if you’re seventeen and the calendar says “last spring.” We had an alibi planted at the parts store—drop in, get seen, leave a receipt trail. Then back to the woods where the day felt bigger than rules.

I didn’t feel guilty. I felt clever. That’s the tell.

On Monday, someone in authority asked where I’d been and the parts store story rolled out smooth. He nodded once and let me go. I walked away with a stomach that didn’t feel like winning.

The lesson took time to name: **systems of authority don’t absolve you from your own integrity**. You can game attendance with receipts and witnesses, but you still have to sit with yourself. The adult rule I use now is straighter: do what you can explain in one sentence to someone you respect.

_AI tie._ I use AI as a risk model and a conscience check. It lays out **narrative risk** (how the story plays) and **data risk** (what the logs will say). Then it asks the one question that kills bad ideas without drama: _“If this shows up on a screen later, are you proud you did it?”_

_Rule: Don’t build alibis. Build choices you can defend._

## **AI Playbook — Integrity Check**

**Purpose:** Align the story you’ll tell with the truth you can live with.

**Prompt:** “For a tempting shortcut, (1) write the one‑sentence explanation you’d tell someone you respect, (2) have AI list narrative vs. data risks, (3) choose the smallest honest action that meets your goal, (4) commit it to a calendar note you’d be fine showing.”

**Constraints:**

- No lies by omission.

- If you can’t say it in a sentence, don’t do it.

- Debrief yourself the next day in 5 lines.
