## The Rule of Charles Cutter

### **Job Never Done Right** :

At 15, I learned perfection was unattainable under my father's scrutiny. His disapproval became my relentless shadow on the farm, where change was constant and praise scarce. My ritual became a meticulous scan of chores done, my mind constantly racing with the question, "What did I overlook?" The best I could hope for was not to be yelled at for messing up. This environment hardens a person’s mind and heart.

I recall when my Dad would drive into the farm, and I would see him looking around, scanning, hoping to identify something that was missed, left open, or not cleaned up. There is something about the deep fear of sometimes violent consequences that one learns ways to avoid.

I remember coming home with a first-place trophy from a wrestling tournament only to be told that I should have done this or I could have pinned the kid. I made mental notes over those years that I would not repeat this and would recognize other’s acco’ accomplishments.

Even in the triumph of wrestling, where victory should have been mine to savor, he found flaws to pick at. I wrestled not just my opponent but my father's impossible standards. A harsh critic to the end, it took 23 grueling years to wrest a single compliment from his lips.

By the time I was 15, I learned that my dad would find something wrong with everything I did, and it was up to me to remember what he said so that he would not have that reason to be updated. This is very hard to do when living on a farm because things are always changing, and there is always something new to do in a new season.

I learned to deal with Chuck Cutter by scanning everything several times to see if there was anything that he would get mad over. I would ask myself, “What did I miss?” I did this with everything. It was a joyous moment sometimes when he returned from the farm and had nothing to say. My dad could drive in and still be sitting in his truck, and he would find something out of place.

**My coping Mechanism was to work harder to prove him wrong**.

Physical punishment in my home wasn't just an occasional discipline; it was a shadow that could darken any day, regardless of my actions. My body learned to absorb blows, and my mind worked tirelessly to understand and evade them. The resilience I built from these encounters was born from a necessity to survive both mentally and physically.

One of the most grueling tests of this resilience came after a wrestling tournament. Crushed by illness and defeat, I faced an even greater opponent—my father's wrath. Outside the Mapleton gym, as sickness overtook me, it was clear that I was in no shape for competition. Yet, this did not sway my father's expectations or correction method. That day's physical punishment did more than leave bruises; it scarred my definition of fairness and justice.

Later, as I sat isolated in the van, watching my brother claim victory, the sense of injustice grew alongside the resolve not to let the cycle of abuse define me and these long hours of solitude turned reflection into a fortress of determination. I vowed never to be the one to wield such power carelessly or cruelly. Physical abuse might have been the language of discipline I was taught. Still, I chose to articulate my life's lessons through a different lexicon—empathy, understanding, and recognition of others' victories.

These experiences, harsh as they were, equipped me with unanticipated armor. Physical pain became the lesser of evils, as I could shield my mind and remain detached, a dissociative tactic that became my secret survival tool. It's a sad realization to prefer the lashes to the eroding effects of emotional negation.

On the day of my first tournament post-accident, my decision to quit was met with a punishment that was out of proportion. It was one of the moments I could never reconcile with, a stark reminder that some actions are unforgivable. Yet, this incident fortified my boundaries—a clear line where discipline became abuse and where I would later draw strength to advocate for myself and others.

Reflecting on my past, the resilience I had forged within me served not just to weather the storms of physical punishment but also to build a mentality that would endure in face of life's many challenges. The mental workaround, as though a crafty navigation through a treacherous emotional landscape, transformed into a kind of mastery. I learned to prefer physical punishment over the insidious nature of psychological harm because I could control my reaction to it—I could dissociate, endure, and recover. It was a perverse form of control, but in an environment where I felt powerless, it was a form of rebellion and autonomy.

This twisted resilience became a foundation for my future. It taught me to withstand hardships with stoic poise and to transform pain into a catalyst for growth. As an adult, I became someone who could take life's hard knocks and stand back up, recognizing the power of acknowledging others' achievements when mine were dismissed. This resolve would later underpin my personal and professional approach to challenges, allowing me to thrive in environments that would have otherwise broken me.

The physical punishments were a dark chapter, but not the entirety of my story. They were the anvil on which my resilience was hammered into shape, and from them, I extracted a fierce determination to change the narrative for myself and for others. I learned to channel the energy from my past, not into bitterness or resentment, but into building a life grounded in positive recognition, personal grit, and the quest for a compassionate existence.

Through these trials, I emerged not with a heart hardened by bitterness, but with one fortified by a clear vision of the type of person I aspired to be—one who could endure, yes, but more importantly, one who could rise above and help others find their path to resilience. While fraught with hardship, the lessons of my upbringing became integral threads in the fabric of my character, stitching resilience into the very essence of who I am today.
