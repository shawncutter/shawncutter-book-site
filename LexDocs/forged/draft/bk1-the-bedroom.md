# The Bedroom

## Two water beds, one door, and everything that mattered.

The door was in the northwest corner. CJ's bed was closest to it — closest to the exit — which made sense if you understood the physics of being brothers. CJ was older and bigger by twenty to twenty-five pounds, and the door was his escape route as much as his advantage. My bed was by the window. I liked the window. I liked the cold.

Two double water beds. The extra wavy kind — lighter stained pine frames with headboards that served as the only shelf space in the room. Not the firm, baffled water beds that eventually replaced the originals. These moved. You rolled over and the whole bed responded. You got used to sleeping on something alive.

On my headboard: a Ferrari Testarossa model and an SR-71 Blackbird replica. Both positioned carefully, the way a kid positions things that represent the version of himself he's building in his head. The Blackbird was the fastest plane ever built. The Testarossa was the car on every poster in every magazine I could get my hands on. I didn't know yet that speed was the thing I'd spend my life chasing in different forms — companies, platforms, decisions. But the objects on the headboard knew.

I built model planes. That was the hobby. Buying the kits, painting the pieces, gluing them together with the focus of someone performing surgery. The room smelled like model glue and whatever the water bed frame off-gassed and whatever CJ had been eating, which at any given time could have been anything.

Four-drawer dresser at the foot of my bed. A chest with "Rocky" on it — because if it had something to do with killing the Russians, I was a fan. Rocky. Rambo. Schwarzenegger. The eighties ran through that bedroom like a power line. We didn't have a lot of channels — ABC and NBC came in clear, CBS was fuzzy, and Fox didn't exist yet in our world — but we had a VCR, and we had Friday nights, and we had Little Caesars from Wooster and tapes from a place called Tapes to Rent that eventually lost its war with Blockbuster.

The TV stood on a tall stand my grandpa built. Nineteen inches, with a VCR spot underneath, and the whole thing nearly reached the textured ceiling. Dark blue carpet on the floor. The ceiling had that popcorn texture that every house in Ohio had in the eighties, the kind that made shadows at night when headlights from a car on the road — rare, but it happened — swept across the window.

I stuck a screwdriver in a wall socket once. The electricity jumped through the insulated handle and hit my funny bone. I remember the jolt, the way my arm went numb and then came back buzzing, the instant understanding that the wall had a secret current running through it that you could access if you were stupid enough to try. I was stupid enough to try most things once. The socket taught me about hidden systems — about the fact that the world has infrastructure you can't see until you make contact with it.

CJ and I fought in that room. Real fights — door-slamming, pillow-swinging, the kind of fights where I had to calculate the physics of being the smaller brother. I slammed him between the wall and the door once, the doorknob catching his side. I loaded an empty shampoo bottle into a pillowcase and swung it like a medieval weapon and knocked him out cold. The rule was simple: hit hard enough that he can't get back up, then run. If he got back up, you lost.

But we also built things in that room. We planned the forts from that room — the oak tree fort with the boards screwed into the trunk and the rope and the platform and the "no girls allowed" sign that we meant with absolute sincerity, inspired by the Little Rascals in both the original and the movie. We were brothers in the way that only works when you share a room with someone: violently, loyally, without much tenderness but with an understanding that went deeper than tenderness.

The cold came through the window at night. I kept it that way — a preference I've carried into every house, every hotel room, every bed I've slept in since. Something about the cold made the water bed feel more like water. Something about the cold made the room feel more like mine.

**Cutter Rule:** The room you grow up in builds the architecture of every room you walk into after.
