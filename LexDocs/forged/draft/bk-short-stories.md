I was trying to figure out whether losing someone suddenly or over time is better. But, I don't think that question needs an answer. In the end, I have lost someone who meant so much, and that cannot be changed. Someone I cared about deeply, who had a hard time showing his feelings, but I know he loved me more than anything. I'd like to share some memories of my dad to illustrate the kind of father he was and what he meant to me, my brothers and sister, and everyone here.

My relationship with my dad was turbulent at times, a sentiment many here today can relate to. From early on, I learned that hard work is required to make something of yourself. Nothing comes free or easy, but you can achieve it with effort. If you were going to do something, you were in it to win it, or as he put it, "kick ass and take names." In second grade, at my first major wrestling tournament, the Ashland JAWS tournament, we were there to win. If my brother CJ and I took first place, we would see Genesis in Cleveland on their "I Can't Dance" tour. We didn't know at the time, but he had already got the tickets. We were going regardless, but it sweetened the victory when I beat my longtime rival, and future friend, Chad Wright. It wasn’t always easy, though; losing was the worst, and you better not cry.

October 29th marked the 20th anniversary of an event that changed my life forever: getting hit by a car. Carina and I were discussing the events that followed the accident. I called my parents to understand their perspective. Learning this information now, as a father myself, holds new meaning. My mom recalled how the EMTs, nurses, and doctors in the ER wouldn't let her see me due to the severity of my injuries. I remember lying on the table, staring into lights, when suddenly my dad appeared, pushing past the nurses in his black and gold Cutter Oil coat and hat. Despite warnings, his determination to see me showed his deep care; he wouldn’t let anyone stop him from seeing his son. I don't recall his exact words, but I remember his strong presence, ensuring I didn't see any fear he undoubtedly felt. Twenty years later, this new information makes me feel good about who my dad was.

I spent a long time in the hospital, and rooms didn't have telephones back then. My dad made sure I had his cell phone, the kind you had to carry in a bag, so I could use it if I needed something. I also spent a lot of time riding in the truck with him as he checked wells and took me to all my doctor’s appointments. Initially, I was in a wheelchair, then crutches. Eventually, I was able to hop tanks on one foot. I hated 210’s. The worst was forgetting the number between the tank and the truck. My dad would get annoyed, a feeling I understood, as he wouldn't forget such things. After the accident, I underwent at least two surgeries a year and countless doctor appointments, most of which my dad took me to. My accident forever altered my life; physical work wasn't in the cards, although I managed to be farm manager for some years. He advised me to use my intellect, my gift, and not to work as hard as he had to. That's what I'm doing now.

During my teenage years, my dad and I didn't get along well. Many teenagers experience this, but for me, it was exacerbated by my rebellion against authority. I got into trouble, threw several parties at the house, but could never escape farm chores. I learned from my dad that you can party, but you must be ready for work. An instance where this lesson played in my favor was on senior skip day. The night before, we partied at Pee Wee Hollow. Knowing I was off school, my dad made me work. The following Monday, Dr. Frame, our principal, tried to catch those who skipped. He thought he had me, claiming a witness saw me at Sterling Farm Equipment that Friday. However, I had the perfect alibi - working for my dad. Shawn 1, Dr. Frame 0.

My dad and I were at odds through most of college. At some point, things changed, though I can't pinpoint when. There was growth on my part and acceptance on his. I realized he had raised me right, and I was turning out OK. I also had to accept that my dad wouldn't always be perfect. He was my dad, and I was lucky to have him. At some point, I began talking to him more than to my mom, sometimes hanging up the phone in disbelief at our conversations. We still argued, but our relationship was one we both cherished. He supported me and offered sound advice when I started my business. He was the first to fly with me when I got my pilot’s license, an experience neither my mom nor wife has shared. I'll always cherish the times we spent together, especially family vacations. These moments taught me the value of time over material possessions. My brother Josh put it well when he said he didn't want presents anymore, just family vacations. I wish we could have had a few more.

A month and a half ago, we went to the condo on a Saturday. I planned to ride the jet ski, return for dinner, and pick up my parents from the airport the next day. The jet ski broke down in a remote area, leading to a two-hour ordeal pulling it through neck-deep water. When I finally made it back, the first person I called was my dad. He asked about the jet ski, what went wrong, and why I didn't take my cell phone. He joked about the situation, saying, "Well, you were calling me, so I knew you were okay." I knew that if I had been truly stuck, he would have mobilized a search party for me. When you needed him, he was there.

We never got to say goodbye or share these feelings. I hope he knew how much he meant to me and to all of us. It's tough to move on, but we will make it; we are Cutters, after all. He left too soon, and it hurts, but he prepared us well. Now, if we mess up, we won’t hear it from him. I would welcome his guidance now.

He taught us a lot, but the most important lesson was his last: Work hard, play hard, and spend time with family. Even if you don't always get along, make it work. Find the time. Leave some work for tomorrow. Because if tomorrow never comes, the work doesn’t matter. All you have is today. Make me proud.

It was the fall of 1997, and I was 17. My older brother CJ lived in the farmhouse with his friends Phipps and Brent. Brent had a pretty badass four-wheeler that I decided I needed. Since I worked on the farm and wasn't paid by the hour, the only spending cash I had came from washing Steve Cherry’s rig down the road once a week, which paid $40. This wasn't enough to purchase a $1,700 ATV. I begged, pleaded, and demanded that I should get that four-wheeler and be given the money to buy it. I could only get big-ticket items if they were approved by mom or dad because of our financial situation.

My mom didn't think I should get it because I got hit by a car when I was 10, and she feared I would get hurt again. My argument was that I had learned from that mistake, and besides, I already used a work ATV on the farm, so what was the harm? It all came to a head one day when I got so angry, I stormed out of the house, slamming the door and screaming, “I could get hurt riding a damn bike!” I went to the farm, did my chores, then took out the four-wheeler, as Brent was a nice guy and let me ride it.

I started out in the north pasture field, where there was a steep hill and lots of mud to tear around in. I was riding with authority, fueled by anger, and needed to vent. Eventually, I went into the other pasture where I knew I could gain more speed. At the far end of the pasture field, I got the idea of ramping where we had just dug out to replace some drainage tile of the barn. All the dirt was piled up on one side. Following the path of the yellow line, it wasn't until I made the left turn and was on final for the ramp that I remembered the barbed wire fence just on the other side of the ditch. I slowed down too much and ended up planting myself directly in the bottom of the ditch, flying over the handlebars.

When I came to, I jumped back on the four-wheeler. My arm folded up in half because both bones were shattered, and there was nothing to support it when the muscle contracted. With my left hand, I reached across, grabbed the clutch, and kick-started the engine.

I then began contemplating, “What the hell am I going to say happened?” as I followed the light blue line around the barns to put the ATV away. When I got to the corner where the line ended, my dad, unbeknownst to me, had just pulled in with the rig and blew the air horn. My heart stopped, and my stomach sank when I saw him sitting there. My arm was resting on my lap because I couldn't hold it up. Now, I had to think of a good story before I reached the barn.

I put the four-wheeler away and walked up through the barnyard, planning to pretend I was checking on the steers and say that I jumped over the gate, slipped on some manure, and fell, catching myself with my arm. It seemed like a plausible story for a broken arm.

By the time I got to the gas tanks where my dad was fueling up the rig, I told him, “I think I broke my arm, I slipped when I was coming over the gate.” It was obvious I had a broken arm, bent at an 80-degree angle. He had to head back out and said, “You have to call your mother. I have to go.” He later said he knew my mom was home and could take me to the hospital, and since it wasn’t life or death, work had to take precedence. Now I had to deal with my mom, who I had just yelled at over the four-wheeler. It felt like justice being served.

My mom rushed up and took me to the emergency room. The doctors questioned my story, doubting an arm could shatter from a six-foot fall. They set my arm, which hurt terribly and never felt right. By evening, it swelled badly. By 2 AM, I had to cut the cast off myself as it had cut off circulation and was too painful. The next day, my dad got me an appointment with Dr. Riley in Akron, who he had a personal relationship with since my car accident. My dad had fixed his oil and gas well.

I ended up needing a plate and five pins in my arm. I was back on the farm in a few days, carrying buckets, and the story stuck. No one questioned it. However, I eventually bragged about it at school, and Brad Wilhelmy heard and told his parents. Since our parents were close, the truth got back to my dad.

Of course, my dad claimed he was figuring it out, as he noticed my arm was on my leg when I pulled around. But if I had been smart, it would have never gotten out. I had to endure lectures from him, my mom, and everyone else for being a fool and not listening to my parents. I guess they were looking out for me, but at the time, it felt like they were holding me back from fun. Damn it, I worked, so I should be allowed to buy a four-wheeler.

I've had a tremendous fear of heights for as long as I can remember. Climbing a ladder more than a few steps was a problem for me. When I was 11, after my parents bought the farm, my dad forced me to face my fear by climbing up the 95-foot silo. There was a safety cage on the silo, and he made me go first. His reasoning was that if I slipped or anything happened, he would be there to prevent me from falling. The most challenging part of the whole experience was reaching the top and having to climb up and over to access the roof.

The fear didn't go away immediately, as I still had to climb back down. However, after that day, my issues with heights vanished. From middle school onward, whenever we were filling the silo, I was the one who climbed it to check how full it was. Since then, I have obtained my pilot's license and even gone skydiving. If it weren't for my dad's push, I would still be afraid of heights. To overcome a fear or an issue, you have to face it head-on. That was how my dad taught me.
