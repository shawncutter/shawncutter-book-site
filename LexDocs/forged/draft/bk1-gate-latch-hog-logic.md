# Forged - Gate Latch, Hog Logic

## The pen break that taught me to design for behavior, not intention.

Ammonia and straw. Squeal and slap. The pig barn at 5 a.m. is a chorus you feel in your ribs. My hands were cold around a metal latch polished by a hundred mornings like this. Move eight hogs through the alley, sort by weight, don’t spook them.

I tugged the gate. It clicked but didn’t set. I knew better and still trusted it. One push from a curious snout, and the hardware confessed. The pen bloomed into motion—pink muscle, low center of gravity, four minds deciding at once that freedom lived in every direction I didn’t plan for.

Noise swallowed thinking. Hogs don’t sprint; they flow. One slid past my knee. Another tested the half-closed panel and found daylight. I made the rookie mistake—more force, louder voice. That escalates everything.

My grandpa used to say, _“Hogs read your elbows.”_ It clicked mid-chaos. I stopped blocking with strength and started drawing with bodies and angles. I opened the wrong gate on purpose to make the right path feel easy. I slowed my feet, lowered my hands, and turned the alley into a funnel I should have built in the first place.

It worked. Not gracefully. Enough.

After we fed and the barn settled, I looked at the latch that faked a click. I’d been designing to my intention—“keep gate shut”—instead of designing to behavior—“curious, strong animals will probe every weakness at nose height.” We replaced that latch with an over-center catch and added a chain. We widened the turn so hogs didn’t jam and panic. We stapled a short panel to hide the shiny hinges that made them stop and stare.

A farm is a textbook written in bruises. That morning edited my copy. Systems that rely on perfect attention will fail at the worst hour. Systems that make the right action the easy action will save you on your tired days.

Later, building Fielding Systems, I recognized the same mistake in software flows. We designed dashboards for how operators “should” use them. They used them like humans use anything: sideways, under pressure, with three other things going wrong. We built guardrails. We created default paths that felt like water running downhill.

Today at EnergiAcres, I do the same with animals, pumps, and people. Gates swing one way. Carts park where they can’t block the aisle. The microgrid reverts to safe when in doubt. _Every_ latch that matters gets a second, dumber backup—the chain that doesn’t care about your theory.

Here’s where AI helps without pretending to be something it isn’t. We build simple agent‑based simulations—the “hog logic” of our operation. We let AI be the wind tunnel for human behavior: Where will carts actually pile up? Which door will someone prop with a rock? Which route will a tired grower take at 9 p.m.? Then we adjust the physical layout and the procedures _before_ the stampede.

AI also watches for drift in real time: an aisle camera counting blockages, a badge reader timing steps between rooms, a small model predicting when a gate will be left ajar based on past patterns. Not to police. To simplify. To make the right path easy again.

_Rule: Design so the right thing is the easy thing—especially when you’re tired._

## **AI Playbook — Behavior‑First Design Drill**

**Purpose:** Redesign any process for how users _actually_ behave, not how you wish they would.

**Prompt:** “Pick one process with frequent mistakes (factory line, onboarding, code deploy, chores). (1) Describe the current path in 7 steps, (2) list 5 user behaviors under stress, (3) identify 3 failure points that invite those behaviors, (4) propose 2 physical or procedural changes that make the right action the easy action, (5) define a simple AI watcher (sensor or log) to flag drift early.”

**Constraints:**

- No fix may depend on ‘more attention.’

- One sacrificial element must exist (your ‘chain’).

- Include a before/after risk score and a 14‑day check.
