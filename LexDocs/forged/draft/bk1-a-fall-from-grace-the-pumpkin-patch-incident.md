# A Fall From Grace: The Pumpkin Patch Incident

In the fall of my thirteenth year, the leaves had just begun to turn, signaling the arrival of autumn. Our farm was a tapestry of orange and brown, the fields readying themselves for the long winter ahead. Among the many projects I had undertaken, my pride and joy was the pumpkin patch I had carefully cultivated over the summer. Each pumpkin was a testament to hours of hard work, a symbol of my growing independence and determination.

Ever since I was a kid, I have had a knack for growing things. A few years earlier, I started planting pumpkins and gourds, and it quickly became a way to earn spending money. I would sell the pumpkins and gourds at the local market and sometimes even to neighbors. It was my little business, and I took immense pride in it because it was how I earned money to spend on whatever I wanted. The success of my pumpkin patch was a source of joy and a way to contribute to the farm uniquely, showing my green thumb from early on.

It was a Saturday afternoon, and I had been busy with chores around the farm. My older brother, CJ, had avoided most of the day’s work and was lounging by the milk house, casually spitting into a pile. It was an act of defiance and laziness, and it didn't sit well with my dad, Charles Cutter, who was already in a foul mood. To my dad, any sign of idleness was unacceptable, especially when there was always work on the farm.

Chuck Cutter was known for his strict discipline and zero tolerance for slacking off. That afternoon, his patience snapped. He stormed over to CJ, yelling at him for wasting time and not pulling his weight. Before long, the confrontation escalated, and my dad, in a fit of rage, began punching CJ. It was a brutal and terrifying scene, one that I had witnessed too many times before.

I was mowing Hughie's yard when Pinky, Hugh's wife, came out and said, "Your dad just beat CJ up at the farm. I don't know how bad it is." This signaled that my dad was on the warpath, and who knew what else would happen. The cause for my dad's rage: a spit pile. That's right, CJ was spitting on a pile, which set my dad off. To my dad, it was a sign of laziness when there was so much work. Did someone have time to spit on a pile?

By the time my dad found me, I saw immediately in his eyes that he was not through. He came right at me as he was taking his belt off. This was the last time I would be whipped with that belt because it did not even hurt. By this time, I had gotten good at blocking pain out when I wanted to. I pretended that it hurt because I figured he would be using fists if he knew it did not break.

But my dad wasn’t done. He was on a rampage. He saw me wearing rubber boots and shorts, which he found infuriating. To him, it was a sign of carelessness, and it pushed him over the edge. He threw me to the ground, ripped off my boots and sweatshirt, and screamed at me about everything I was doing wrong. The boots were hurled across the road into a cornfield.

I was still on the ground when I saw him climb onto the Oliver 550 tractor, a machine symbolizing our farm's hard work and perseverance, and I was grabbing my boots just as he headed straight for my pumpkin patch. My heart sank as I watched him drive over my carefully tended plants. He did donuts, tearing up the soil and crushing the pumpkins I had nurtured all summer. I can still picture the dirt and mud being slung around. Each pass of the tractor was a blow to my spirit, destroying something I had created with my own hands.

In those moments, as I watched my dad destroy my pumpkin patch, I felt a mix of anger, sadness, and helplessness. This wasn’t just about the pumpkins; it was about the countless hours I had spent working on something that mattered to me, something I had built from the ground up. I had done the work and tended the garden all season. My dad’s actions were a harsh reminder of the control he wielded and the unpredictable wrath that could instantly undo months of hard work.

But I didn't let that destruction define me. I salvaged as much of the garden as possible and planted new pumpkins the following year. My dad’s friends got wind of the incident, and they made a display that made him look like a real asshole among his friends because of how crazy he was. They saw what I had put into that patch and couldn’t believe he had done such a thing.

This incident taught me about resilience and the importance of continuing to move forward, no matter how many setbacks I faced. It was a lesson that would serve me well in the coming years as I faced other challenges and learned to rebuild repeatedly. The pumpkin patch was just one of many trials that helped shape me into who I am today.
