# The Yard

## She's done enough. But she doesn't know how to stop.

My mother is sixty-seven years old and she is still blowing off the yard.

Not a normal yard. Not a suburb. Acreage — real acreage, the kind of land that takes the better part of a morning to mow and the better part of an afternoon to maintain. Beth Cutter is out there on the mower, in the garden, trimming, clearing, working the edges of a property that has been in her family — the Bechtels — since Ohio was a state. She's been doing this her entire life. She doesn't know any other speed.

I want to tell you about my dad. I've written a lot about Chuck — the belt, the truck, the speed, the intensity, the softening at the end. I've written about what he built and how he pushed and what it cost. I could write about Chuck for years and never run out of material, because Chuck was a weather system. You couldn't be in the room with him and not feel it.

But my mother.

My mother is the reason any of us survived the weather.

Beth Cutter has an eternal love in her heart. I don't mean that as poetry — I mean it as engineering. Whatever mechanism exists inside a person that allows them to keep loving through damage, through betrayal, through years of absorbing what someone else couldn't hold — that mechanism in my mother was built to industrial specification. It does not wear out. It does not take days off. It does not require reciprocity to function.

She was the protector. When Chuck's discipline crossed the line between formation and something else — and it did, more than once — she was the wall between us and the worst of it. Not always successfully. Not always in time. But she tried, and the trying mattered more than I understood as a kid.

What she had to endure. I can say that now because I've had enough distance to see it clearly, and enough therapy to stop pretending the distance was normal. She endured Chuck's intensity, his infidelity — Shawna exists because of what he did — his expectations, his temper, his brilliance, his death. She endured all of it and she came out the other side still loving every one of us without condition.

Unconditional love is a phrase people use so often it has lost its weight. Let me put the weight back. Unconditional love, in practice, means: I will put your needs ahead of mine until it becomes pathological, and then I will keep doing it because I don't know how to do anything else. That's Beth. She puts others first to her own detriment. She always has. She would give you the last thing she had and then apologize for it not being more.

I'm forty-six years old now, and I'm sitting on the same farm where she grew up, where the Bechtels have been for two hundred years, and I can see her from where I'm writing this. She's in the garden. Of course she's in the garden. It's March in Ohio and the ground is barely thawed and she's already out there because that's what she does — she tends things. She tends land. She tends people. She tends whatever is in front of her.

She's done enough.

I say that the way a son says it when he finally understands what his mother gave up so he could become whatever he is. She's done enough. It's time for the kids to step up. It's time for us to tend her the way she tended everything else.

I don't know if she'll let us. Letting someone take care of you requires a muscle she never developed, because she was too busy developing the one that takes care of everyone else. But we're going to try. I'm going to try. Because the woman in the garden at sixty-seven — still mowing, still clearing, still working the edges — that woman built something more durable than anything Chuck or I ever constructed.

She built people who come back.
