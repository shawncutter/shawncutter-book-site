# The Hilltop

## You couldn't see through it. You were in it.

In the summer, the canopy closed over completely. You couldn't see through it from anywhere on the property — you were just inside it, the way you're inside a sound. The leaves came all the way down to the driveway's edge. The light came through in pieces. If you were from the city you might have said it was beautiful. We just called it home.

In October, before the leaves fell, the canopy went spectacular. That last turn before the house came into view — you'd come around it in October and the whole thing was lit up, orange and gold and deep red, the kind of color that Ohio does once a year and then puts away. It was genuinely stunning. Still is, in memory.

Our driveway was a quarter mile long. It made a ninety-degree turn at the bottom, then climbed the hill at a pitch steep enough to make your mother get a running start. In winter it iced over and became something else entirely — not a driveway but a problem, a daily calculation. You had to pick your path on the way down to the bus. Pick wrong and you'd slide. The ice didn't care that you were a kid trying to get to school.

I spent years of my life navigating that driveway on crutches.

Not a week of it. Not a month. Years. Forty surgeries worth of recovery cycles, and in between surgeries there were always crutches, and the crutches always had to meet that hill in whatever weather Ohio decided to send. There was no accommodation. No ride to the bottom because I was in a recovery phase. You just worked out how to do it. You picked your path, you tested your weight, and you got down or you got up, and then you did it again the next day. I don't know if this built something specific in me or just revealed something that was already there. But I know what it taught: the hill is not going to change. You change.

Come spring thaw, that driveway had to be reclaimed. The freeze-thaw cycles would wash out the gravel, rut the surface, leave it cratered and scarred. So I'd take the tractor out — the farm tractor, not some small machine — with a box scraper on the back, and I'd scrape it smooth. Pull the gravel back up from where it had migrated down to the road, grade the surface flat, do it section by section. A kid learning to manage a piece of land. You don't just live on a property like that — you maintain it, or it takes itself back.

Winters you'd salt the whole thing and sometimes that still wasn't enough. Sometimes the car would stop halfway up and you'd have to decide — back down an icy hill in the dark, or stay and dig. We dug. That's what Cutters did.

At the top of that last turn, the house came into view on the left. Ranch style — nearly four thousand square feet of it, wide and low, sitting on top of everything, surrounded by forest. There was a trampoline in the front yard for most of the years I can remember. A big front door nobody ever used. Everyone came in the door next to the garage, all the way on the right side of the house — that's where the concrete was, that's where the garage door was, that's just what you did. The part that stuck out from the house was the office, and it overlooked everything. That's where Chuck's chair was.

The house was loud. Not just yelling — the particular volume of a family who lives spread across a property large enough that voice is the only technology you've got. We got ABC and NBC clear, CBS came in fuzzy, and Fox didn't come in at all. So most of our entertainment was each other, for better and worse.

Chuck broke a phone in half once. The portable phone. He got pissed because it would ring — sometimes it was the business line, and somebody had to pick it up and take a message, and nobody wanted to do it. So he grabbed the portable phone and broke it over his leg. Snapped it clean in half. When he finally came back out and sat down in his chair, we were all just in silence, waiting. And I picked up the bottom piece, handed it to Kristin, and said, *You talk. I'll listen.* The whole room died laughing. That was how you survived in that house — get to the joke before the room decided to go the other way.

We had fights — CJ and I, mostly, over food, over territory, over the ancient and unresolved war between older brothers and younger brothers. Kristin and I had a mutual interest in managing CJ. That alliance kept us both alive.

Amish buggies were part of the road. You'd get behind one and pace yourself. You didn't lay on the horn. They were neighbors and neighbors in the country are different from neighbors in the city — they're a fact, same as weather. When we needed extra hands for big farm work, a whole Amish family would come out. Father and several of the boys. Good workers. They watched us the way we watched them — curious but not rude about it.

During bad storms, a man named Hugh Palmer would come up the hill.

Hugh lived in a trailer down in the valley. When the weather turned serious — the kind of serious that made a man in a low-lying trailer think about water rising — he'd come up to the house. That was his way of visiting. That's how we got to know him. Storm by storm, season by season, he became a fixture, and then he became more than a fixture. He became the grandpa figure in my life. Not assigned, not official — earned. Through showing up during bad weather and staying long enough for it to become something else.

There are people in your life whose role doesn't have a name, who exist outside the official family structure, who fill something in you that the official structure left empty. Hugh was one of those people for me. I don't know if he understood that. I don't know if those things are ever understood at the time.

Friday nights, almost every Friday night: Little Caesars from Wooster and a couple of VHS tapes from Tapes to Rent. Blockbuster hadn't made it to Wooster yet. We'd get the pizza in the orange-and-red box, still hot from the drive, and we'd watch two movies. That was the rhythm. During winter the days ended early, and I read in my room. We eventually got a Nintendo. I was better than CJ at everything on it, which I regarded then and regard now as a foundational fact about the world.

The woods started where the yard ended and they went deep — deep enough that if you went far enough in, your parents couldn't see you and your voice could carry all the way across the valley to our cousins' place. We'd yell and they'd yell back, which was the whole point. You didn't need them to come over. You just needed to know they were there.

That's what the hilltop was. Not a landscape. A system. Everyone knew their range, their volume, their role. We were loud and we were spread out and we were always somehow accounted for.

I didn't know any of this was unusual until I left.

**Cutter Rule:** You don't choose the ground you're forged on. You just learn its weight, its weather, and how far your voice carries.
