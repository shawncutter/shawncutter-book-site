# Forty Dollars and a Fork

## My first lesson in margins came from a man in a pickup truck at a pumpkin fair.

I was a farmer before I was anything else, which means I was a businessman before I understood what that word meant.

Country Acres was what Cracker Barrel would be if Cracker Barrel had been founded in rural Ohio by people who actually lived there. It had a restaurant, a shop out front that sold decorative things — gourds, dried corn, knickknacks, seasonal stuff. My aunts ran it, which meant I had a distribution channel before I had a word for that either.

The deal was simple: I grew gourds and pumpkins on our land, brought them in, and they sold them. Fifty percent to me, fifty percent to the house. Every week I'd go back, check the display, fill it back up. A check would appear.

This was, for a kid in rural Ohio with limited options, real money. The only other income stream I had was washing semi trucks for the loggers down the road — they went out into the woods, destroyed their equipment with mud, and I cleaned it back up for $40 a week. That was big money then. The gourds were more consistent. The gourds scaled.

I was competitive about my pumpkins. I grew them large and I grew them right and the year I entered one at the Wayne County Fair, I was aiming for first. I got third. Third at the Wayne County Fair in a pumpkin competition is a real thing, a genuine outcome, a ribbon, but it was not first and I was not satisfied.

What happened next taught me more about business than any class I ever sat in.

A man came up to me after the judging. He'd gotten second place. He told me the pumpkin I'd brought was exactly what he was looking for, and asked if I had more like it. I told him it was the biggest one I had — and then I told him the truth, which was that I had a truck full.

He said he'd take all of them.

He came out, loaded everything I had, and left. I had cash in my pocket that felt enormous.

Then I found out what he did with them. He drove them to Cleveland and sold them. More than tripled his money.

I just stood there with my check and my third-place ribbon and did the math. He hadn't grown a single pumpkin. He hadn't watered anything, staked anything, hauled anything to the fair. He'd shown up on the day of the transaction and he'd known something I didn't: the same pumpkin that was worth one dollar in rural Ohio was worth three dollars in Cleveland.

Location. Timing. The gap between what something costs to produce and what someone will pay to receive it. He hadn't cheated me. We'd made a deal. He'd just seen the full board and I'd only seen my piece of it.

That was the day I started asking *then what?* after every transaction. Not just what is this worth to me right now, but what does the person who's buying it do with it next? What does it become? Who sees it in the next zip code?

The pumpkins were still the pumpkins. The difference was always the map.

**Cutter Rule:** Know the full chain before you price the first link. What it's worth to you is never the only number that matters.
