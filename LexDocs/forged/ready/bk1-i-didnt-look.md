# I Didn't Look

## The chaos chain only goes so far. Then it gets to the part that's on me.

For thirty-five years I've traced the same chain.

The pin fell out of the wagon hitch. I lost twenty minutes. The gas tank was empty, so I lost another thirty I would have spent filling cans. Without the wagon I could drive twice as fast. So I did.

I've drawn this chain on whiteboards. I've told it in rooms. I've laid it out the way an engineer lays out a failure mode: the pin was the initiating event, the gas was the amplifier, the empty wagon was the accelerant. If any one of those three things doesn't happen, the timing shifts. The lady on Rice Hill Road is already past the driveway. I don't see her. I go home. I make it to the pizza party the next day. Forty surgeries never happen.

It's a clean chain. There's an elegance to it. It distributes the weight across events instead of decisions.

Here's the problem with it: I've been telling it wrong for thirty-five years.

The pin, the gas, the wagon — those are real. They happened. But here's what I said out loud recently for what might be the first time:

*I didn't look.*

Not the pin. Not the gas. Not the wagon. I was going too fast. I was thinking about the next thing and the thing after that and never once about the most immediate thing — the road, the turn, what might be on the other side of it. I was ten years old going full speed and I did not look left before I turned.

That's what happened.

I'm not saying this to punish the ten-year-old in the story. He didn't deserve what came next. No kid does. But there's a version of carrying this event that treats me as purely a victim of physics and bad timing, and there's a version that treats me as someone who made a choice — maybe the only choice I made the whole afternoon, among all those random events stacking up — and that choice was to not slow down.

Two different stories. Same scar.

The engineer in me prefers the chain because chains are systems and systems can be fixed. You can change the pin design. You can add a backup gas supply. You can put a hitch guard on the wagon. The chain is learnable.

But the other story is learnable too, and it's the one I actually need: *speed is not the same as urgency, and the next thing is not always the most important thing.*

I've run that same pattern my whole life. Always moving toward what's next. Always thinking three steps ahead of where my feet are. It's served me well in business. It's cost me things at home. It almost cost me everything on a road in rural Ohio on October 29, 1990.

The chaos chain is real. I'm not retiring it. But it ends at me, not at the pin.

*I didn't look.* Three words I needed thirty-five years to say.

**Cutter Rule:** Trace the chain as far back as it goes. Then own the last link. That's the only one you can change in the next version of the story.
