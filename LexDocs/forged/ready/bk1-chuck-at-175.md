# Chuck at 175

## He had been broken by a motorcycle. He taught us to ride them anyway.

The accident happened before I was old enough to understand it, so I learned it the way you learn a lot of things in that house — as background, as inherited fact. Chuck Cutter had been on a motorcycle at dark. Someone had left a broken tractor in the road. He hit it. It destroyed his right knee. He spent three months in a hospital.

That was the story. That was the summary.

What I didn't understand until much later was what that experience did — or didn't do — to his relationship with velocity. Because the thing about Chuck was that the motorcycle had tried to take something from him, and his response was to make sure his children started earlier. Not cautiously. Earlier.

I was three years old when I got on my first dirt bike.

Mine was a 60cc Yamaha 3-wheeler. CJ had an 80cc 4-wheeler. Chuck was the teacher — not the kind of teacher who stands at a safe distance and points, but the kind who shows you firsthand and expects you to do the thing. Before I could ride a bicycle. Before training wheels were even a consideration. The first form of motorized transport I learned was a 3-wheeled dirt bike in the hands of a man who had been hospitalized for a motorcycle accident and decided the lesson was: *teach them earlier, teach them better.*

We raced. And we won trophies.

This is the part of the story that still gets me, because it implies a whole world I lived inside as a little kid — organized races, other kids and their fathers, the physics of small vehicles at speed, and Chuck in the background doing whatever Chuck did when he was watching us compete. Quiet and intense. Managing the situation. Probably already calculating the next variable. We brought home trophies from that world, which means there was a level of actual skill involved, which means the 3-year-old on the 60cc Yamaha learned to do the thing right.

Then there was Michigan Speedway.

We got him ten laps for Father's Day, through a company called Track Time. That was supposed to be an occasion. It became a habit. We got so close to the owners of Track Time that Chuck looked into buying the whole operation — saw a business in it, saw a life in it, saw a way to turn the thing he loved into the thing he ran. The 2008 housing crisis and recession ended that conversation before it fully started. But for a window of time, Chuck Cutter was genuinely considering becoming the man who owned the track.

He ended up with 175 laps instead.

That's not casual. That's not someone who tried something and checked it off. That's someone who said *this is mine* and kept going back, lap after lap, visit after visit, until the number got large enough to mean something. I've done fifty myself. I know what the track feels like at speed — how everything narrows to a line, how the grandstands go peripheral, how the noise changes from inside versus out. I understand now what kept pulling him back.

Here's the thing I only understood in hindsight: when I got hurt — the accident, the forty surgeries, the years of reconstruction — my dad and I found a place that only the two of us occupied. We were both people whose bodies had been permanently changed by a single moment. We'd both been through the long tunnel of hospitals and pain and coming back to something. That's not a common club. That's a very specific club.

Chuck never talked about this directly. That wasn't his method. But I could feel it — the particular way he looked at my recovery that was different from how anyone else looked at it, because he knew what it was, from the inside, and he knew that you don't get to come out the other side unchanged, and he was watching me figure out which version of myself I was going to be.

He'd already figured out his answer: you go faster.

I'm still working on mine. But I'm fifty laps in, and I intend to close the gap.

**Cutter Rule:** The thing that hurt you is not a reason to stop. It's a curriculum. Learn it completely. Then teach your kids before they're old enough to be afraid of it.
