# Chuck at Sixty Seconds

## The portrait I carry.

His hands were strong. Not the trained strong of someone who works out — the permanent strong of someone who has gripped things his entire life. Wrenches, rigs, fence posts, steering wheels. His hands looked like tools.

His voice was deep in the way that comes from a pack of Marlboro Reds a day since the age of nineteen. Not a mean voice. Just a voice that had been seasoned by decades of smoke and cold mornings. When he said your name you knew he meant it.

He walked with a limp. If you didn't know to look for it, you might miss it. But it was there — his right knee, shot by an accident when he was sixteen, the same age I would be when I put my arm through a gate and shattered both bones. He never talked about it much. It was just the way he walked.

He wore the same outfit almost every day in the summer. Shorts and a black shirt. Calvin Kleins. He wore a hat constantly — pink with black lettering, black with pink lettering, other people's hats for a while — and eventually just Cutter Oil hats, because that was who he was and there was no point pretending otherwise.

He was a practical joker who sometimes took it too far. At restaurants he'd josh the waiter and I'd watch the room and try to read whether we were getting thrown out. He could turn a charged moment funny if he got there first, which is a gift I recognized in him and borrowed. He was always slightly on the edge of something — the room never fully settled when he was in it.

He got softer as the years went on. Less edge. More patience. I think he was figuring out that the methods his own father had given him were too blunt for the world his kids were inheriting. He was adapting. He would have kept adapting, if he'd had more time.

He had a belt method. He explained it to me during one of the beatings, which I've always considered a very Chuck thing to do. If you put your hands up to block, it didn't count toward the number. You were supposed to take them. You could cry all you wanted, but hands down. There was a structure to it, a logic, a rule. This was the same man who explained deal terms while negotiating on the phone and expected me to follow along from the passenger seat.

He said *use your intellect* more than he said almost anything else. He said it when sports came up and it was clear from the context of my foot and my surgeries that the physical version of a thing wasn't available to me. He said it when I was inspecting my own foot, looking at whatever the latest damage was, doing the thing you do when you're trying to assess your own body the way a mechanic assesses a car. He'd say it without a lot of fanfare. Not a speech. Just the sentence.

Most of the time when he said it I wasn't looking at his face. I was in the passenger seat and he was driving. The Ohio fields going by outside the window. The radio on low. His cell phone mounted permanently in the dash — he had one of those before almost anyone we knew. I'd listen to him work a deal and try to guess where we were going next based on whatever I'd overheard him say on the last call.

That's how I learned to read a room. Sitting in the passenger seat, listening, trying to predict. It was a game I made up because I didn't have much else to do, and it turned out to be the most useful game I ever played.

He was my first case study in how a man moves through the world when he means what he says.

**Cutter Rule:** Watch how someone's hands move before you listen to what their mouth says. The hands already told you the real story.
