## THE DIAGNOSIS

### What You Have

The Forged arc is your largest and richest — but it's also the messiest. 28 stories, nearly 39K words, with significant overlap and duplication. The material splits into three layers:

| Layer | Stories | Words | Feel |
|-------|---------|-------|------|
| **Long-form chapters** | 3 stories | ~21,600 | Detailed memoir drafts — Chapter 1, Short Stories, Early Life Continued |
| **Polished vignettes** | 8 stories | ~4,400 | Tight, voice-forward pieces — Gate Latch, Silo Steps, Pumpkin Patch, Senior Skip Day |
| **Seeds / fragments** | 17 stories | ~12,700 | Rough notes, duplicates, technical pieces misplaced from Foundry |

**The main problem:** The long-form chapters (Chapter 1, Short Stories) are written in a more distant, literary voice — almost a third-person memoir style. The vignettes (Gate Latch, Silo Steps) are written in your *real* voice: direct, sensory, Feynman-style. The vignettes are dramatically better. The long chapters contain gold but need to be broken apart and rewritten in your true voice.

**The second problem:** At least 6 stories are misclassified — they belong in Foundry or Forging (Private Infra First, Pads We Can Ship, Microgrid, Permit Ledger, etc.). These are EnergiAcres technical pieces tagged "forged" incorrectly.

### What's Strong

- **The ice cube story** — Chuck's belt, 5-year-old Shawn picking rocks. This is your origin scene. Raw, unforgettable, and it sets up everything about your father.
- **Silo Steps** — Perfect structure. Fear → exposure → transformation. This is the template for your best writing.
- **Gate Latch, Hog Logic** — Farm-to-systems-thinking bridge done right. Sensory opening, clear lesson, practical application.
- **The Short Stories collection** — The eulogy to Chuck, the ATV crash at 17, the tattoo story, the boat story with Mom. These are *gold* but buried in a 14K-word dump file.
- **Senior Skip Day** — Great comedic voice. Shows the cleverness that becomes a theme.

### What's Missing

1. **Mom's story** — She barely exists in the arc. Who was she before the accident? What was her relationship with Chuck? What did she teach you that was different from what he taught?

2. **Wrestling** — Listed in every theme array but never gets a dedicated story. Wrestling is referenced as a metaphor constantly. Where's the story about the mat, the weight cut, the coach, the loss that shaped you?

3. **The sensory farm** — You have analytical pieces about farm lessons, but where's the *poem* of the farm? The smell of silage at dawn. The sound of the tractor in fog. The feeling of mud in boots. A single "day on the farm" story would anchor everything.

4. **Pre-accident innocence** — Most of the childhood stories are told through the lens of lessons learned or danger survived. There's almost nothing about just *being a kid*. Joy without foreshadowing. Playing without a moral at the end.

5. **The Amish neighbors** — You grew up 45 minutes south of Cleveland in Amish country. That's a fascinating detail that never gets explored. What was that like? How did Amish culture influence the Cutters?

## MORNING RECORDING PROMPTS — FORGED ARC

### WARM-UP: THE SENSES OF THE FARM

1. **Close your eyes and go back to the hilltop.** You're standing at the top of the quarter-mile driveway. What do you see? What do you hear? What does the air taste like in summer versus winter? Walk me through the property like you're giving a tour.

2. **The sounds of the Cutter house.** You said everyone in the neighborhood would hear the Cutters. What did that mean? Were there fights? Laughter? Machinery? What was the volume of your childhood?

3. **Amish country.** You grew up 45 minutes south of Cleveland in Amish country. What was that like day-to-day? Did you interact with Amish families? How did their world brush up against yours?

4. **Two channels and fuzzy CBS.** What did you actually DO in the evenings? Before the accident. No internet, limited TV. What filled that time? What was the rhythm of a winter night in that house?

### CHUCK

5. **Describe Chuck Cutter in 60 seconds.** Physically. His hands. His voice. The way he walked. The hat. The coat. Not the lessons — the man. What did he look like coming through a door?

6. **The belt method.** You wrote about the counting, the "hands up don't count" rule. Tell me more about that system. When did you first understand it wasn't random — that he had a *method* to discipline? How old were you?

7. **Chuck laughing.** You've written a lot about Chuck's anger and discipline. When did he laugh? What made him actually happy? Was there a version of Chuck that was light?

8. **"Use your intellect."** After the accident, your dad told you to use your brain, not your back. That's one of the most important sentences in the entire book. When exactly did he say it? Where were you? What did his face look like?

9. **The truck rides.** You spent a lot of time riding in the truck with Chuck to doctor appointments and checking wells. What happened in that cab? What did you talk about? What did the silence sound like?

### MOM

10. **Tell me about your mom.** She barely appears in the book so far. Who is she? What was her relationship with Chuck like? What did she teach you that was different from what he taught?

11. **Mom driving up the icy hill.** You wrote about her getting a running start at the driveway. That's a great image. Tell me more moments like that — Mom in motion, Mom being resourceful, Mom being fierce.

12. **Mom at the hospital.** After the accident, the EMTs wouldn't let her see you. She followed the squad to Wooster. What do you know about that drive? What has she told you about it since? As a parent now, what does that feel like to imagine?

### THE FAIR AND THE FORTS

13. **Wayne County Fair — the BEST day.** Not the mischief (you've got that). Tell me the single best day at the fair. The peak. What did winning feel like? What did the fairground smell like at night? What was the drive home like?

14. **The forts in the woods.** You built forts with trip wires and booby traps against the Russians. Close your eyes and walk me through the best fort. What was it made of? Where was it? What did it feel like to be inside it?

15. **Your first business.** Growing gourds and selling them at Country Acres. How old were you? How did you come up with it? What did your first sale feel like? What did you do with the money?

### WRESTLING

16. **The mat.** Wrestling is in every single theme list in the entire book but has no dedicated story. Take me to a match. The smell of the mat. The weight of the singlet. The sound of the whistle. What did wrestling teach you that nothing else could?

17. **The loss.** You wrote "losing was the worst, and you better not cry." Tell me about a specific loss. Who beat you? What happened in the car ride home? What did Chuck say — or not say?

18. **Chad Wright.** You beat him at the Ashland JAWS tournament to earn the Genesis tickets. You later became close friends. Tell me about that relationship. What's the arc from rival to friend?

### BEFORE THE ROAD

19. **October 28, 1990.** The day before the accident. What do you remember? Was it a normal day? Can you reconstruct it? What were you looking forward to — the pizza party, the championship celebration?

20. **The last normal thing.** Before the accident, what was the last thing you did that was just... normal kid stuff? No foreshadowing. No lesson. Just being ten.

21. **If the pin hadn't fallen out of the wagon.** You've traced the chaos chain — pin falls, no gas, no wagon means you drive fast. You've analyzed it like an engineer. But have you ever just *sat with it* emotionally? Two seconds. What does that do to a person over 35 years?
