## THE DIAGNOSIS

### What You Have

The Fractured arc is your most emotionally charged material — and the most structurally complex to organize. 17 stories, ~23K words, covering the period from the accident at age 10 through the long surgical recovery, high school rebellion, depression, loss of faith, and eventual emergence into college.

| Layer | Stories | Words | Feel |
|-------|---------|-------|------|
| **The Accident Sequence** | 5 stories | ~10,900 | Multiple versions of the same event — Day the Earth Shook, Prologue, 87 Stitches, Surgeon's Revelation |
| **Recovery & Identity** | 5 stories | ~6,100 | Painful Rebirth, Wounded Soul, Phoenix Rises, Depression, Therapy/Loneliness |
| **Reflections & Meta** | 4 stories | ~3,700 | 30-Year Echo, Rolling Ohio Hills, Initial Feedback, Family/Farm/Oil influence |
| **Misplaced / Technical** | 3 stories | ~2,000 | Boring Wins Winters, Un-Birthday Mansfield, Master Question List |

**The main problem:** You have written the accident scene at least FOUR times (Chapter 2, Prologue, 87 Stitches, Surgeon's Revelation) — each from a slightly different angle but with massive overlap. This is actually good news: it means the event is burning to be told, and you have multiple drafts to pull from. But the arc needs ONE definitive version that incorporates the best elements of each.

**The second problem:** The recovery/identity material is mostly analytical — written *about* the experience rather than *from inside* it. "Painful Rebirth" reads like a therapy intake form. "Phoenix Rises" reads like an outline. The exception is "Therapy, Loneliness, and Clarity" which is your strongest piece in the arc — raw, present-tense, honest.

### What's Strong

- **The accident scene in Chapter 2** — The raw, unpolished version. The pin falling out. The wagon. The lady's face through the windshield. Time slowing down. The sock coming off. Blood squirting with each heartbeat. Dad pushing past the nurses in his Cutter Oil coat. This is the most powerful material in the entire book.
- **Therapy, Loneliness, and Clarity** — Your best writing in this arc. The "lonely/unsafe vs lonely/clean" framework is original and powerful. The midnight kitchen scene with your wife.
- **The 30-Year Echo** — The 20th anniversary reflection. The idea of revisiting the accident site as an adult.
- **Rolling Ohio Hills** — The sensory return to the place.
- **The ATV crash at 17** (in Short Stories) — Shows the pattern repeating. Brilliant comedic storytelling with real emotional undercurrent.

### What's Missing

1. **The hospital as a world** — You spent months in Akron Children's. You mention counting ceiling tiles, befriending nurses, the playroom you couldn't reach, the morphine drip. But there's no immersive "life in the hospital" piece. What was a day like? What were the nights like? Who were the other kids?

2. **The first steps** — Your timeline says "1992: First steps after hospital — fell after 1.5 steps." That's an INCREDIBLE scene waiting to be written. Where were you? Who was watching? What did the floor feel like?

3. **The depression named** — "Breaking Barriers" is 458 words and barely scratches the surface. "Wounded Soul" is 689 words of outline. Depression across the surgical years needs a real, named, inhabited story — not a summary.

4. **High school Shawn** — The rebellious teenager. The parties at the house. The tattoo. The cigarettes. The anger. You have fragments but no unified piece about what it was like to be the kid with 40 surgeries in a normal high school.

5. **Loss of faith — the full story** — You mention telling the pastor at age 10 that God doesn't exist. That's a pivot point. But it's one paragraph. What did faith look like before? What did its absence feel like during the surgery years?

6. **Grandpa's death and the nipple rings at the funeral** — This is in the timeline but has no story. That sounds like a quintessential Shawn Cutter story — grief and rebellion in one frame.

## MORNING RECORDING PROMPTS — FRACTURED ARC

### THE ACCIDENT (Build the definitive version)

1. **The morning of October 29, 1990.** Before the ATV. Before the road. What do you remember about that morning? School? The soccer championship? What was the weather like?

2. **CJ's decision.** Your brother insisted you go get gas alone while he waited for Mom and McDonald's. Have you ever talked to him about that? Does he carry anything about it?

3. **The pin falls out.** The wagon hitch pin. You've described the chaos chain. But I want the sensory version: What did it sound like? What did you see? What was the dirt road like? Walk me through the decision to leave the wagon and ride fast.

4. **The lady's face.** You saw her face through the windshield. You've written it multiple times. Close your eyes and describe it one more time — but this time, tell me what she looked like. Have you ever found out who she was? Did she carry anything from that day?

5. **Dad in the ER.** The Cutter Oil coat. Pushing past the nurses. You said it was the first time you saw fear in his eyes. Stay with that image. What did his hands look like? Did he touch you? What did he say — the words, if you can remember them?

### THE HOSPITAL

6. **Waking up.** You woke up two days later in Akron Children's ICU. You tried to go back to sleep hoping it was a dream. What was the first sound you heard? What was the light like? What was the first thing you looked at?

7. **The gauze.** The wet gauze pads drying on your foot, then being pulled off with the dead tissue. You wrote that you passed out from the pain. I need you to go there one more time — not for suffering porn, but because this is the moment that taught you pain is survivable. What did you hold onto (physically and mentally) during those moments?

8. **A day in the ward.** Walk me through a full day — morning to night. When did nurses come? What was breakfast like? What did you stare at? When did you cry? When were you bored? What did you watch on TV? What time was the worst?

9. **The playroom.** You finally got a wheelchair and made it to the playroom, then were disappointed because there were games you could have asked for. Tell me about that moment. What games were there? Who else was in there?

10. **The other kids.** Were there other kids on the ward? Did you make friends? What were they in for? What happened to them?

### IDENTITY & DEPRESSION

11. **1.5 steps.** Your first steps after the hospital — you fell after a step and a half. Where were you? Who was watching? What did the floor feel like? What did falling feel like, knowing you'd just gotten back on your feet?

12. **Back at school.** The first day back. What was that like? Did kids stare? Were they kind? Did you feel different? When did you realize you were now "the kid who got hit by a car"?

13. **The depression — when it first got a name.** You write about depression in the outlines but always at a distance. Take me inside it. Not the clinical description — the feeling. What time of day was worst? What did it make you want to do? What did it make you not want to do?

14. **Gamifying surgery.** You wrote "getting a surgery was the thing I just had to go through, over and over, so how could I gamify it?" Tell me about that. What were your tricks? What did you tell yourself before going under? Did it get easier with each one, or harder?

15. **The rebellion.** Parties at the house. Cigarettes. The tattoo. Getting in trouble at school. Was that about pain, or was it about proving you were alive? Or both?

### FAITH AND LOSS

16. **The pastor at age 10.** You told a pastor that God doesn't exist from your hospital bed. What do you remember about that conversation? What did his face look like? Did anyone else hear you say it? What did it feel like — liberating? Terrifying? Nothing?

17. **What replaced God?** After you rejected faith at 10, what filled that space? Philosophy came later (Nietzsche, Stoics). But between 10 and college, what did you believe in? Just work? Just anger? Was there ever a moment you reached for faith again and it wasn't there?

18. **Grandpa's funeral and the nipple rings.** This is in the timeline. Tell me the story. What happened? Was it rebellion? Grief? How did the family react?

19. **Dad's accident.** Chuck had his own accident at 16. Everyone in the family knew about it. When you were lying in the hospital at 10, did anyone draw the parallel? Did Chuck? Was there a moment of recognition between you — "I know what this is"?

### THE BRIDGE OUT

20. **The moment it shifted.** When did the Fractured period start becoming something else? Was there a day, a conversation, a decision where you thought "I'm not just surviving anymore"? What turned the corner?

21. **What the surgeries actually gave you.** Not the scars, not the pain tolerance — the thing nobody talks about. What did 40 surgeries give you that nothing else could have? What's the gift inside the fracture?

22. **If it hadn't happened.** You've traced the chaos chain of the accident a hundred times. But have you ever imagined the Shawn who didn't get hit? Who went to the pizza party the next day and grew up normal? What do you think happens to him? Is he better off or worse?
