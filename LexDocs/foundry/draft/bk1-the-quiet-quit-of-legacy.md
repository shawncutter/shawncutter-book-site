# The Quiet Quit of Legacy

## When your last name stops being fuel and starts being friction 

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/b33c7bd9-c488-45d5-8f5c-732eeb4c2d50-RackMultipart20251130-156-xy024d.png)

**Excerpt:** I grew up believing Cutters were always on the same team on the outside. Cedar Valley Farms proved we weren’t reading the same playbook. I had to choose which parts of the legacy to keep—and which to quietly let die.

At a family thing years back, someone said, “The Cutters always show up.” It landed like armor on my shoulders. Pride. Identity. A clean story.

By the time we were in the thick of Cedar Valley Farms, that same line felt like sandpaper. We weren’t “always showing up.” We were barely hanging together. The Cutter name stopped feeling like a banner and started feeling like a stuck costume.

Legacy had shifted from **fuel** to **friction**.

### Different houses, different myths

From my angle, the Cutter Standard was simple: do the hard thing, don’t quit, fight inside but present a united front outside. My older brother and I lived under that. Same father, early version. Hard, demanding, fear-based. We internalized: we are a unit in public, no matter how rough it gets in private. You bleed, you back each other, then you go fix the fence.

My younger siblings grew up with another version of him—a man changed by age, pressure, regrets. Less structure. More chaos. They saw cracks we weren’t allowed to see. The myth of “one team” was already broken by the time they were old enough to notice.

So when I invoked legacy during Cedar Valley Farms—“We’re Cutters, we don’t phone it in, we hold the line”—I thought I was calling everyone back to a shared standard. To them, it sounded like control dressed up in nostalgia.

They quietly quit the version of legacy I was trying to enforce.

### When legacy makes you the enemy

In the heat of running CVF, my attachment to “Cutter” made me push harder. I wasn’t just trying to build a farm. I was trying to redeem a name. Fight back against decay. Prove that the stories about who we are still meant something.

So I enforced accountability. Pushed for clear roles. Called out sloppy work. Insisted that being family didn’t earn you immunity from standards.

They heard: “You care more about the business than us.” I felt: “You’re tearing down the thing we stood on.”

We were both right from where we stood.

At some point in that spiral, I realized I was burning real relationships to defend an idea of “Cutter” that only existed in my head and my brother’s. The legacy we were actually living—the one with fractured trust and contested narratives—didn’t match the banner I was waving.

So I did something I never thought I’d do: I **quiet quit my own legacy script.**

### Legacy, refactored

I split it into three columns:

- **Keep:**

- **Drop:**

- **Rewrite:**

I stopped using the last name as a cudgel. I stopped chasing siblings with speeches about who “we” are. I started focusing on who **I** am, and who my kids see when they look at me.

Legacy became something I **demonstrate** , not something I demand. If my daughters grow up thinking “Cutter” means strong, honest, shows up, owns his mess, loves fiercely, that’s enough. I don’t need to make everyone in the extended family sign that charter.

Some relatives will keep quiet-quitting my version of legacy. That’s fine. They’re allowed to. Drift is data, not drama.

My job now is simple:

- Keep the parts of the name that load my frame and help me build.

- Let the parts that rub raw quietly die with me.

- Hand my kids something lighter but still strong enough to build on.
