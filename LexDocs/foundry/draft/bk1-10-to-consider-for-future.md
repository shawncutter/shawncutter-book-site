# 10 to consider for future

these are full of hullucinations

## 1) When Your People Don’t Want the Dream

**How to keep loving them without converting them**

Saturday morning. Pancakes. I came in hot about a site control win for Terrasow. The table was warm, the room wasn’t. My wife poured syrup. My daughter fed the dog under the chair. I floated “combined heat and power” across the table like a balloon. Nobody grabbed the string. The balloon hit the ceiling and died.

They love me. They don’t want the dream. That used to feel like betrayal. It isn’t. It’s simply a different appetite. They didn’t audition to join my mission. They signed up to live with me.

I stopped recruiting at home.

New rules: **Ask, don’t announce.** If they open the door—“How’s the project?”—I share. If they don’t, I don’t wedge it in. **Hold a bridge hour daily.** I’m Dad, partner, human. No slides, no “quick update.” **Send a lighthouse message weekly.** Three lines in the family chat: what I built, one win, one non‑work note. Signal, not a sales deck. **Celebrate their worlds first.** Ten minutes on their day before I mention mine. Cheap fuel with compounding returns. **Protect the home from the storm.** Investors get my pitch. Family gets my presence.

It still aches. Missions want converts. But love isn’t go‑to‑market. The dream is loud enough at work. At home I keep my voice low and my hands busy.

**Cutter Rule:** Don’t turn love into a pipeline. Protect it from your weather. **Field Test:** For 7 days, share work _only_ when asked. Send one three‑line lighthouse message. Log the difference in mood at dinner.

## 2) The Long Winter of Building

**Why momentum feels like loneliness in disguise**

January, 5 a.m. Breath turns to fog in the shop. The deck says “momentum.” The calendar says “silence.” No one claps for trenching conduit. No one likes your 47th revision of a PPA. Winter isn’t cold. It’s quiet.

Momentum is not noise. It’s compounding in the dark. The long winter of building is the gap between effort and echo. If you mistake the gap for failure, you’ll torch good work just to hear something.

I changed how I measure motion.

**Micro‑metrics:** inches, not miles—one signed term sheet, a passed pressure test, a greenhouse heat loop holding temp overnight. **Peer check:** one builder call each week. No pitching. Just “what held / what failed.” Loneliness shrinks under shared specificity. **Daily anchor:** same mug, same chair, same 10 lines in a build journal. If nothing moved, I moved. That counts. **Quarterly story:** write the winter narrative like a farmer: what we seeded, where we pruned, what survived the freeze.

Spring comes. It always does. The trick is not ripping out the roots because the field looks empty in February.

**Cutter Rule:** Momentum is quiet until it isn’t. Trust the compounding, not the crowd. **Field Test:** Pick 3 micro‑metrics you can touch daily. Track them for 30 days. No vanity counts. Only moves that would embarrass you if they were false.

## 3) Scar Tissue as Strategy

**Pain maps patterns. Read yours before you hire, raise, or scale**

My shoulder aches before big hires. Phantom pain. The body remembers what the mind edits. Last time I ignored it, I hired charisma without competence. Paid twice: cash and morale.

I started a **Scar Map** —not poetry, procedure.

**Event → Trigger → Overreaction → Countermeasure.**

- **Event:** investor ghosted post‑diligence. **Trigger:** scarcity panic. **Overreaction:** over‑promise timelines. **Countermeasure:** written “no hero dates” policy; two‑stage timeline with buffers.

- **Event:** early exec betrayed trust. **Trigger:** charm = safety. **Overreaction:** skip reference calls. **Countermeasure:** back‑channel references; 90‑day scorecard before equity vests.

Before I hire now, I run the Scar Map across the req. Before I raise, I list my tells: speed, flattery, certainty. Before I scale, I stress‑test the brittle spots—cash concentration, single‑point skills, my need to be liked.

Scars don’t make you weak. Unread scars do.

**Cutter Rule:** Unread pain turns into policy by accident. Read it and write it on purpose. **Field Test:** Build a one‑page Scar Map from your last three failures. Add one countermeasure per row. Use it on your next hire or raise.

## 4) The Cost of One More Rep

**How “just one more” corrodes trust at home**

“Be right there.” I said it with one eye on a “quick email.” Ten minutes later bedtime was over. My son was asleep with a book on his chest. The email didn’t ship anything. It just fed my need to feel necessary.

“One more rep” is founder creatine at work and acid at home. Micro‑betrayals stack. You don’t mean harm. You still teach that your people come second to your dopamine.

I put a governor on the habit.

- **Ban the phrase.** “One more” is a tell. If I hear it, I stand up.

- **Commit / Comply / Cancel.** If I commit to a time, I either comply or I cancel out loud 30 minutes prior with a re‑promise and a make‑good.

- **Visible timer.** A dumb kitchen timer ends loops my brain would keep open forever.

- **Arrival ritual.** Keys in bowl, phone in glove box, two minutes to wash hands. My body learns the job changed.

Trust is built in the boring minutes you don’t steal.

**Cutter Rule:** “One more” now equals “three less” later—less trust, less respect, less joy. **Field Test:** Run a 14‑day **No One‑More** challenge. Every time you almost say it, stop instead. Track misses and make‑goods. Compare mood at home.

## 5) Bridge Hours

**A daily 60‑minute ritual that keeps a life from splitting**

6:30–7:30 p.m. The hour has a name. **Bridge.** I walk in, put the phone in the glove box, and cross.

**Scope:** be a person, not a founder. Play cards. Chop onions. Sit on the floor. One screen: a movie together if we choose it together. **Rules:** start on time, end on time; no “updates”; if work is an emergency, I say it before the hour starts. **Menu:** walk the block; “show me your day” (they pick the topic); five‑minute stretch; two‑minute story from my childhood; ten‑minute cleanup race.

Predictability beats grand gestures. The bridge doesn’t fix distance. It keeps the river from carving a canyon.

**Cutter Rule:** Presence protects more future than performance at home. **Field Test:** Block 7 Bridge Hours this week. Put the phone in the car. Log: start, end, what you did, and one sentence on how the room felt at minute 40.

## 6) Founder as Shock Absorber

**Keep the load path clean: investors, partners, team, family**

An investor fired off a 10 p.m. “urgent” note. I felt the jolt hit my hands, then my jaw. The easy move was to pass the shock downstream—ping the team, pace the kitchen, bleed the house. The right move was to absorb it and route it.

I drew the **Load Path** on a whiteboard: **Market → Investors/Partners → Me → Execs → Team → Home.** Arrows only move upstream for decisions and downstream for clarity. Panic never moves downstream. Not once.

**Absorb:** I read, I breathe, I journal in bullets. What is signal? What is someone else’s cortisol? **Route:** I answer upstream quickly with facts, not apologies. I give the team the next clear step and the why. I give home nothing but my presence. **Dissipate:** coach call, cold shower, kettlebell swings, a page of writing. Energy has to go somewhere. Choose the steel, not the people.

You are the bushing. Wear is part of the job. Replace yourself with systems as you grow, but until then, be the rubber that saves the frame.

**Cutter Rule:** Absorb shocks in your body and systems, not in your people. **Field Test:** Draw your Load Path. After the next jolt, write where the energy went. If any hit home, fix the routing by tomorrow.

## 7) Letters I Don’t Send

**Writing to the people I’m disappointing on purpose**

I keep a folder: **Unsent.** Four types:

**To my wife:** _I know I’m choosing work this quarter. I see what it costs you. Here’s the hour and day I guarantee. Here’s the date the valve turns._ **To my kids:** _This isn’t about loving you less. It’s about building something I believe helps other families. Ask me anything. I’ll answer without slides._ **To a friend drifting:** _I miss the easy days. I can’t carry both of our schedules. If we find each other again, I’ll be here._ **To my dad and the kid I was:** _Thank you for the grit. I’m done needing the jab to move. I’ll carry the load without the voice._

I write them, then wait 72 hours. Most stay unsent. The heat drops and the truth gets simpler. Sometimes I send a distilled paragraph. Never the whole purge. The letter was for me. The sentence is for them.

**Cutter Rule:** Vent in private; decide in public; love in person. **Field Test:** Write one unsent letter tonight (10 minutes, no edits). Wait 72 hours. Pull one sentence that is both true and kind. Send only that—if sending serves them, not you.

## 8) The Dinner Test

**How to tell if your company is eating your kids’ childhood**

I used to miss dinner “by a little.” The little added up. Childhood doesn’t invoice you. It just ends.

The **Dinner Test** is simple:

- **Seven dinners a week.** You owe four.

- **Interruptions allowed:** one. If a true fire, step away, name the fire, return with a new time.

- **No phones on the table.** A phone face‑down is still a phone at dinner.

If I can’t clear four dinners, the company is chewing the wrong material—usually poor delegation, unclear priorities, or my hero habit.

When I pass the test, everything else gets easier: better sleep, fewer “one more reps,” less resentment. When I fail twice in a week, I run a **brownout protocol** —drop noncritical projects for seven days, win back the table, then rebuild the schedule.

**Cutter Rule:** If dinner is negotiable, childhood is. **Field Test:** Track dinners for two weeks. Score yourself honestly. If you hit fewer than four, trigger a 7‑day brownout and publish the plan to your family.

## 9) The Quiet Quit of Friends

**They’re not wrong to walk. You’re not wrong to stay**

At a reunion I felt invisible. Not malice—mismatch. They left my lane. I left theirs. We both had good reasons.

I stopped making it a trial. I started sorting without shame:

- **Origin friends** (history).

- **Process friends** (love the grind).

- **Result friends** (love the wins).

- **Soul friends** (sit with you in silence).

As the build deepens, the circle narrows to process + soul. The rest get updates, not obligations. When a friend quiet‑quits, I send one last clear note: _I love you. I’m deep in a build. If our roads cross again, I’ll be happy to see you._ Then I stop dragging the past into the present.

Letting go isn’t arrogance. It’s alignment.

**Cutter Rule:** Let drift be data, not drama. **Field Test:** List your top 10 friends. Label O, P, R, or S. Send one clean love note to a drifter. Book one walk with a process friend.

## 10) After the Exit

**Why money doesn’t return what absence took**

The wire hit. The email came. Everyone clapped. Monday was quiet. My calendar looked like a field after harvest—stubble, wind, nothing to hold.

Money turns down some alarms and turns up others. It won’t return missed dinners, lost friendships, or the version of you that wanted applause more than intimacy. After my exit, I felt rich and empty at the same time. Meaning debt.

I paid it down like a builder: **Sabbatical with purpose.** Not a vacation—a projects list with sweat: fix fences, coach a kid’s team, rebuild a tractor, learn to weld better. **Small builds only.** No new empires for six months. Prove I can be inside my own life without a conquest. **Service block.** One day a week working for someone who can’t pay me back. Skill in service rethreads the soul. **Therapy stays.** Winning doesn’t close old loops. It exposes them in silence. **New scorecard.** Sleep, dinners, calls to my mom, hours in the garage with a kid, strawberries in January because the pump we wired works.

Exits clear the yard. They don’t plant it. You still have to bend, dig, water, and wait.

**Cutter Rule:** Liquidity solves money problems. Only presence solves meaning problems. **Field Test:** Write a one‑page **Post‑Exit Job Description** : your role, weekly cadence, and the five numbers you’ll measure that have nothing to do with dollars.
