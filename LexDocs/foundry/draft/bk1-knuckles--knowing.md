# Knuckles & Knowing 

## _Shop-Class Epistemology_

A bolt tells the truth faster than a man. Lean on it wrong and it strips. Overtighten and it snaps. Miss alignment and the whole system hums wrong until it fails somewhere expensive. That was my first classroom: farm, oilfield, shop—heat, weight, noise.

Dad mocked the word “philosophy.” Fair. He didn’t need a label to know what worked. I did. After ten, I needed a way to sort what was real from what was just loud. Pain makes noise. So does praise. Philosophy became testing—epistemology if you want the textbook word. How do you know you know? Not “how do you feel?”—how do you demonstrate it.

Descartes gave me permission to doubt. Useful until doubt becomes a lifestyle. Aristotle kept me out of the fog: name the thing, its form, its purpose, its causes. That clarity saves hours—with plumbing and with people. Nietzsche got me moving when the body wanted to quit. Sometimes the right play is rest; sometimes it’s pick up the yoke anyway. The Stoics taught me to stop arguing with weather and other people’s fear. Rand reminded me that agency isn’t a sin and sacrifice dressed up as virtue is still sacrifice. When all that collides, responsibility decides.

Method, not mood. Same test across code, crops, and contracts:

- Heat: can the idea take friction without excuses?

- Weight: can it carry a real load—ten customers, a real invoice, a week of uptime?

- Repetition: can we run it three different ways and get the same outcome?

If not, it’s opinion. Opinions are fine. They just don’t get to run my life or my company.

I keep my hands dirty for calibration, not nostalgia. Soldering reminds me patience beats pressure. The five-gallon carry reminds me leverage beats heroics. Fixing a cross-thread with a tap and die reminds me there’s a tool for almost every mistake.

Those rules translate to meetings, to parenting, to 11 p.m. loneliness when the house is quiet and I’m trying to tell if the ache is a warning or a tax I chose. Dad’s jab still echoes: “What’s your philosophy on this?” Here it is—simple. Start at the edge of what you can control. Test small. Scale only what survives heat, weight, and repetition. Don’t bet the home on your mission. Don’t let mood outrun method. If you don’t know, say so—and get back to the bench.

Cutter Rule: Knowing = predictive power under pressure. If it can’t predict, it isn’t knowledge.

Field Test: Take one belief about your work, your body, or your relationship. Write a 24-hour prediction that could be wrong. Tomorrow, check it. Adjust your belief toward what actually happened.

Shawn Index (0–10): Clarity • Utility • Grit.

Epigraph: “We are what we repeatedly do.” (Attribution debated; the spirit stands—and belongs on the wall of a shop.)
