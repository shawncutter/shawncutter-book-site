# The Fork I Almost Missed

## _I almost chose the mad scientist path. The universe would have course-corrected me anyway._

I keep going back to the Shawn from three or four years ago. Not to critique him — more to study him, the way you'd study a photo of yourself at a younger age and try to figure out what was behind your own eyes.

The Shawn at Corum. The Shawn on that call. The Shawn at the dinner table, physically there, somewhere else entirely. I play those scenes back now and what I notice is how much he didn't know he didn't know.

That's the trick of it. Once you learn something, it's like — _oh, this was always here_. The whole emotional dimension. The sensory layer stacked on top of the cognitive one. The thing where you're not just processing a moment, you're inside it with the people you love, and their signal is coming through clean instead of getting filtered through whatever system you happen to be optimizing that week.

I didn't have that on a personal level. I didn't know I didn't have it.

---

A few years ago I was at a fork. Either I was going to go to the family thing and make it work, or I was going to be done with it. Not dramatically. Quietly. Full mad scientist. Shut the door, run the lab, let the rest drift.

I want to be honest about that, because softening it loses the point. I am still, on some level, more comfortable alone. That hasn't gone away. The work-on-yourself literature would tell you it's supposed to — that you reach some milestone and the aloneness stops being the default setting and becomes a preference you visit. I'm not there. I don't know if I'll ever be there.

But I'm experiencing more now than I used to.

Extra moments with the kids. Disney. The specific draw of a kid sitting close to you — the physical pull of it — which three-years-ago Shawn wouldn't have registered as real data. These are the moments that carry on. They plant into the rest of our lives like seeds, and the ones I'm planting now are the ones my kids will grow around when I'm not in the room.

So I'm trying to plant as many as I can.

---

I'm writing this on a Tuesday morning in April. I have a wicked long day ahead — landowner meetings, maps, acreages, the Mansfield work. And here I am at my desk crying about my kids, not really wanting to be talking about it, but I think I should be talking about it. If I talk through it, I get through it. I get through it either way. But you know what I mean.

---

If I could do it over, I would have coasted. A couple of years. Really let myself fall back in love with coding. Quiet mornings, long blocks, one problem at a time. No agenda except the work itself.

I would not have gotten so into other people's shit.

That's the honest answer, and it took me a long time to say it out loud. I took on other people's fights, other people's seasons, other people's dramas, and I did it under the banner of building something together — which was true on paper and false in my body. The body keeps the real score. Mine was running hot for years, and I kept mistaking the heat for progress.

But I think I needed to get there. I needed to grind against that particular wall long enough to see that the wall wasn't going to move. And in the wreckage of it, I saw — actually saw, with the emotional apparatus I'd barely been using — that the thing I was supposed to be building wasn't over there in the fight. It was right here in the house.

One way or another, the universe was going to make me arrive at this. Whether I chose it and saw the fork coming, or whether I didn't see it and life course-corrected me — the destination was the same. All my awareness bought me was the path.

---

I've been building my whole life. The body. The companies. The case for why any of it meant something. I figured if I stacked enough of those, the legacy would hold.

The part I wasn't building was the emotional one. The personal one. The muscle I didn't know was a muscle.

I'm just getting started on that work. Not finishing it — starting it. At forty-six, with kids who notice when I'm present and when I'm not, and a wife who has already been watching me closely for years and waiting for me to catch up.

Lots of work ahead.

The landowner meeting starts at nine.
