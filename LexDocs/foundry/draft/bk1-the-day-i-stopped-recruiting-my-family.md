# The Day I Stopped Recruiting My Family

## Love isn’t a go-to-market motion 

 ![Image](https://lex-img-p.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/img/fd3dd9aa-5b00-44be-a55a-769712be3662-RackMultipart20251130-150-dr4r3g.png)

**Excerpt (Medium):** Cedar Valley Farms nearly broke us. I was funding the whole thing and still became the enemy. That’s when I stopped recruiting family into my mission and started protecting the relationships from my weather.

Harvest at Cedar Valley Farms felt like combat. Tractors running on fumes. Dryers screaming all night. Everyone half-awake, sticky with dust and resentment. The barn smelled like diesel, sweat, and stale coffee. We got through it—barely. Yields in the bin, checks incoming, no one seriously hurt. On paper, that’s a win.

It didn’t feel like one.

The team was cooked. My siblings were fried. We had pushed hard through the most stressful stretch of the year, and instead of relief, we hit revolt. I started talking next steps—how to improve workflows, where we’d been sloppy, what we needed to tighten before the next season. I thought we’d earned the right to talk about getting better.

They heard something different: attack. Criticism. Ingratitude.

I’m wired to win. At all costs. Family included. If you’re in my way, I’ll find a way around or through. That wiring built companies. It also made me the villain in a family business **I was funding myself**.

### When family wants special treatment—and you want a standard

The first real fracture came in a post-harvest “debrief” that turned into a cage match. I laid out misses on a whiteboard: late starts, unforced downtime, errors we could prevent next year. I tagged owners to each one so we knew who’d fix what.

One sibling crossed his arms. “You act like we’re just employees,” he said. “We’re family. We busted our asses. Maybe ease up.”

Another nodded. “You’re not our boss. You’re our brother.”

Technically, I was both. Financially, I was also the only one all-in. I had real money in the dirt, real risk on the line. They wanted family rules when it came to accountability and business rules when it came to payout. They wanted the upside of my wiring without the pressure that comes with it.

I tried to explain: The standard has to be the standard. Harvest doesn’t care whose last name is on the mailbox. The weather doesn’t care that we’re related. Either we build a professional operation or we cos-play a business until the market kills us.

They didn’t want to hear it. To them, accountability felt like betrayal.

That’s when the roles flipped. In their story, I became the problem: the brother who “only cared about the business,” who wouldn’t give family a break, who treated them like staff. I was still paying the bills. I was also now the enemy.

### Different fathers, different homes, different legacies

Part of the fracture came from something I didn’t want to admit: we did not grow up in the same house, not really.

Same address. Different father.

The version of my dad I got was hard, fear-based, demanding. The rule for me and my older brother was clear: Cutters are always on the same team on the outside. We handle our fights in the barn, not out in town. Out there, we’re a wall.

By the time my younger siblings came up, he’d softened in some ways and twisted in others. They saw more chaos, less structure. Less of the “we are one unit facing the world,” more of the fractures. They didn’t inherit the same unspoken rulebook I did.

So when I said “We’re Cutters, we hold a standard, we don’t fracture in public,” it hit them like propaganda from a country they never agreed to join.

I was fighting for a legacy they never actually bought.

### The decision: stop recruiting, start protecting

After that debrief blow-up, I walked the fields alone. Corn stubble under my boots. Sky going orange. My chest felt tight—part grief, part rage.

I realized I’d been running one big mistake: I was **recruiting family into my mission** the way I recruit executives, partners, investors. Pitch deck. Vision. Standards. I thought if I sold the dream harder, they’d change their wiring.

They wouldn’t. And it wasn’t fair to expect it.

So I made a decision that felt like ripping out a root: I would **stop recruiting my family**.

- I would stop trying to turn siblings into operators they never wanted to be.

- I would stop selling the mission at Thanksgiving.

- I would stop tying my self-worth to whether they signed up for my standard.

Practically, that meant three moves:

1. **Separate seats:** Family is family. Operators are operators. If someone is in both seats, we write a real job description and a real scorecard. Otherwise, they’re in the “family only” column.

2. **Warrior hiring:** When Cedar Valley Farms needed killers—people wired to win the way I am—I went outside the bloodline. Real warriors who weren’t strapped to old dogma.

3. **Relationship triage:** If it came down to saving the business or saving the relationship, I chose the relationship. I didn’t like it. It was the right call.

I also had to get okay with the ultimate burn: **I might lose the business anyway.** I had to be able to look in the mirror and say, “If this fails, it won’t be because I wrecked my family trying to force them into my wiring.”

### From recruiting to witness

Once I let go of conversion, my job at home changed:

- I stopped pitching. I started modeling.

- I stopped dragging them into my habits. I doubled down on them myself: sleep, training, steps, movement, therapy.

- I stopped needing them to validate the mission. I focused on being someone worth being around when the mission turned off for the day.

Some noticed and adjusted. Some didn’t. That’s their path. My work is to keep my side clean.

Inside Cedar Valley Farms, I stopped calling in favors based on blood. If you’re in the operation, you meet the standard. If you can’t, we move your seat or you exit the org—even if we share DNA. Out there, we’re a team or we’re not. At home, we’re family either way.

That’s the line now.

**Cutter Rule:** Don’t conscript your family into your war. Protect them from your weather and hire warriors elsewhere. **Field Test:** Make two lists: “Family” and “Operators.” Move every person into exactly one for the current season. Have one hard conversation where you stop expecting someone in the Family column to behave like they’re in the Operator column.
