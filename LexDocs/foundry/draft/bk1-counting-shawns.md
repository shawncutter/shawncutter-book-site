# BK1: Counting Shawns

Dad would say it with a grin that wasn’t a grin: “What’s your philosophy on this?” He meant stop thinking and turn the wrench. On the farm, truth was torque, angle, heat. If it worked, it was true. If it snapped, you were wrong. End of sermon. I went looking for something bigger than torque. Church didn’t hold. Friends didn’t hold. So I started molting. I gave myself numbers to track the skins.

Shawn #5 was grunge and pissed. #9 wore black and wanted to disappear. #11 ironed his shirts and practiced smiles in the mirror. Each version made a trade: fit in, survive, win. None were home. Under the costumes: a kid ripped open at ten who learned that pain is private and performance pays. I didn’t have a language for that, so I borrowed one. Nietzsche for the will when the body quit. Sartre for responsibility that doesn’t wait for permission. Descartes for the right to doubt what everyone swore was solid. Aristotle for sorting parts before touching them. The Stoics for guardrails when panic wanted the wheel. Rand for the reminder that agency isn’t a sin. I tried on groups like jackets—bands, teams, study circles, companies. If they clapped, I stayed. If the room turned, I molted. Costume changes keep you alive. They also blur your face. The gift: your hands keep grabbing the same tools. Mine: take it apart, label it, rebuild it cleaner, and make it testable. That’s what philosophy became for me. Not quotes. Not posture. A way to test reality without breaking the part that matters most. Epistemology with grease. Heat: can this idea take friction without excuses? Weight: can it carry ten customers, a real invoice, a week of uptime? Repetition: same outcome in a different light? If it holds, keep it. If it fails, learn where and why. Here’s the part I rarely said out loud: the people closest to me often didn’t meet the new version when it arrived. They held onto cached Shawns—#5, #9, #11—long after I’d moved. That’s on me, too. I iterate in silence. I ship the update and forget release notes. I ask them to guess who walked in the room. They guess wrong. Then everyone pays—me for being unseen, them for loving a ghost.

Vulnerability means owning the communication gap. I am not who I was. And I don’t always signal the change. So I’m learning to publish what changed and what didn’t: what I believe now, what I’m testing, what you can count on, what’s still in draft. Not a performance. A field guide. The worst seasons were when I tried to be a single idea. All Nietzsche, all will: burnout. All Stoic, all acceptance: stone. All Rand, all agency: isolation. The trick isn’t to marry a school; it’s to marry a method: Diagnose with doubt. Decide with responsibility. Execute with will. Recover with acceptance. Audit with first principles. That’s the loop. Dad’s jab still echoes: “What’s your philosophy on this?” Here it is. Start with the part in your hand. Identify the load path. What is this thing meant to carry? Name what you can actually control.

Name where it shears, leaks, or inverts. Then run a single test. One move, one measure—not a speech. If it holds, scale it. If it fails, thank it and try again. Truth shows up when it has to carry weight. I kept counting Shawns into my thirties. Then something shifted. After a meeting where we inched a power campus forward—technically a win—I felt the old itch to audition a new version for applause. I laughed instead. The count didn’t matter. The method did. I wrote on the whiteboard: Stop auditioning. Start integrating. Then I went inside and washed dishes. That’s vulnerability in the small: let the people near you see the update while it’s still buggy. Say what’s changing before it’s perfect. Invite them to hold you to the method, not the mask.

Philosophy didn’t make me special. It made me useful to myself. It turned sadness into structure and loneliness into a load I could carry without making other people carry it for me. It also made me accountable to the people I love: less guessing, more saying. Release notes, not reinvention.

Cutter Rule: If a belief can’t survive heat, weight, and repetition, it isn’t knowledge—it’s costume. Communication Rule: If the people closest to you can’t describe the update, you haven’t shipped it. Field Test: Write “Release Notes vNext” to one trusted person. Four bullets: What changed, what stayed, what you’re testing, how they’ll know it’s real. Ask for a read-back in their own words. Adjust. The Shawn Index: #5 wanted to be seen. #9 wanted to disappear. #11 wanted approval. Today’s Shawn wants the work to hold and the people he loves to feel safe. That’s enough. Epigraph: “What is needed is, not the will to believe, but the wish to find out.” (Sources disagree on wording; the spirit stands.)
